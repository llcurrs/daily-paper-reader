## Abstract 

We investigate variational quantum classifiers (VQCs) for land-cover classification from multi-spectral satellite imagery, adopting a feature-map perspective in which the quantum circuit defines a nonlinear data embedding while the readout determines how this representation is exploited. Us-ing the EuroSAT-MS dataset, we perform a systematic one-vs-one evaluation across all class pairs under a controlled experimental protocol, comparing classical baselines (logistic regression, SVMs, neural networks) with VQCs employing both linear readout and quantum-kernel SVM strategies. Our results show that, while VQCs with linear readout do not outperform strong classical baselines such as RBF-SVM, the same trained quantum feature map can significantly improve performance when reused within a kernel-based decision framework. A qubit-count sweep further reveals sat-uration effects consistent with the mismatch between exponential Hilbert space dimension and linear parameter scaling. Overall, our findings highlight that the effectiveness of quantum models depends critically on the interplay between representation and readout, and that meaningful gains may arise from combining learned quantum feature maps with classical decision mechanisms rather than seeking direct replacement of classical models. 

I. INTRODUCTION 

Land-cover classification from multispectral satellite imagery is a fundamental task in Earth observation, supporting applications such as environmental monitoring, urban plan-ning, and land-use mapping. A wide range of supervised machine learning methods have been applied to this problem, including support vector machines (SVMs), logistic regression, and, more recently, neural networks (NNs). In particular, deep learning has emerged as a dominant paradigm for remote-sensing image analysis due to its ability to learn expressive representations from large-scale data, albeit often at the cost of increased model complexity and computational requirements [1]. In this context, an open question is whether quantum machine learning (QML) methods— currently constrained by a limited number of qubits and shallow circuit depths—can provide

Rather than aiming for outright perfor-mance gains over strong classical models, it is more realistic in the near term to examine whether variational quantum classifiers (VQCs) can learn meaningful feature representations under such constraints, and how the choice of downstream decision mechanism influences their practical effectiveness. VQCs provide a compact parametric alternative to NNs, mapping classical inputs into quantum states through parametrized quantum circuits (PQCs) [2]. Recent theoretical re-sults have characterized the expressiveness of such models, supporting their interpretation as expressive nonlinear models in high-dimensional feature spaces [3]. From a machine learning (ML) perspective, VQCs can therefore be viewed as learned nonlinear feature maps, where the resulting representation can be exploited by different decision mechanisms, including direct measurement-based readouts or kernel-based classifiers [4–6]. Here, rather than positioning VQCs as replacements for classical models, we adopt a more conservative and practically motivated view: the underlying PQC serves as a nonlinear feature transformation, whose effectiveness depends on how the induced representation is utilized. This perspective enables a direct and controlled comparison with classical linear and nonlinear baselines under identical preprocessing, while also isolating the role of the downstream decision rule. In this work, we conduct a comprehensive one-vs-one evaluation across all 45 class pairs of the EuroSAT-MS land-cover benchmark [7, 8]. To ensure a fair and controlled comparison, we adopt fixed data splits and repeat training over five independent seeds, with random-ness affecting only the optimization process. Results are aggregated at the class level to characterize relative task difficulty and model behavior across land-cover categories. Our objective is not to claim quantum advantage, but to analyze the quality of the representations learned by PQCs and to examine how their effectiveness depends on the downstream decision mechanism under realistic simulation constraints, in line with known advantages and limitations of quantum machine learning [9]. The main outcomes of our modeling and numerical analysis can be summarized as follows: 

• We perform a fully controlled pairwise evaluation across all 45 EuroSAT-MS class pairs, ensuring consistent preprocessing, fixed splits, and multi-seed training for all models. 3• We show that a compact PQC provides a nonlinear feature representation that con-sistently improves over linear models and remains comparable to a shallow NN on the same inputs. 

• We demonstrate that the effectiveness of the learned quantum representation depends strongly on the choice of readout, with an SVM-QK (trained) systematically outper-forming the standard linear measurement head derived from the same trained circuit. 

• We show that the quantum kernel induced by the trained circuit closes much of the performance gap to an SVM-RBF, indicating that the learned feature space captures meaningful similarity structure. 

• We analyze the effect of circuit size through a qubit-count sweep, showing that per-formance improves with increasing qubit count before gradually saturating under the considered architecture.  

> II. RELATED WORK

Land-cover classification from satellite imagery has been widely studied using both classi-cal machine learning and modern deep learning methods. Traditional approaches often rely on spectral or reduced-dimensional features combined with classifiers such as logistic re-gression, random forests, and support vector machines. In particular, SVMs remain strong baselines in remote sensing because they can perform well with limited labeled data and can model nonlinear decision boundaries through kernels such as the radial basis function (RBF) kernel. With the growth of labeled remote-sensing benchmarks, deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs) became dominant for land-use and land-cover classification, in-cluding on EuroSAT, where deep CNN baselines were evaluated on Sentinel-2 imagery [7]. Larger-scale datasets such as BigEarthNet further enabled training and benchmarking deep models on multispectral Sentinel-2 image archives [10]. More recently, transformer-based and self-supervised approaches have become increasingly important in remote sensing. Vi-sion Transformer and Swin Transformer models have been applied to LULC classification with transfer learning and fine-tuning [11], while masked-autoencoder pretraining methods such as SatMAE explicitly exploit temporal and multispectral structure in satellite imagery [12]. Geospatial foundation models such as Prithvi-EO further represent the current trend 4toward large pretrained models that can be adapted to multiple Earth-observation tasks [13]. In contrast to these large classical models, our study focuses on compact PQC-based fea-ture maps and compares them against lightweight but carefully controlled classical baselines under identical preprocessing and data splits. Within quantum machine learning for Earth observation and remote sensing, prior work has explored kernel-based, hybrid quantum–classical, and quantum-inspired approaches. Quantum SVM methods have been investigated for remote-sensing data classification, in-cluding quantum-kernel and quantum-annealing formulations [14–17]. Circuit-based hybrid quantum NNs and quanvolutional models have also been applied to Earth-observation im-agery, including multispectral and satellite-image classification tasks [18–22]. Related work has further examined quantum hyperparameter selection, practical bottlenecks, and the broader potential of QML for remote sensing [23–27]. In parallel, quantum-inspired archi-tectures based on unitary or Hilbert-space transformations have been proposed for multi-spectral satellite classification [28]. Compared with these studies, our focus is not only on end-to-end VQC accuracy, but on interpreting the trained PQC as a learned nonlinear fea-ture map and evaluating how different readout mechanisms, namely a linear measurement head and a trained quantum-kernel SVM, exploit the same learned representation.

The design of each of these components can significantly affect model performance and has been the subject of extensive research. In particular, the choice of data encoding is known to strongly influence the expressive power of variational quantum models [30]. In this work, we focus on the representation produced by the circuit after training. Specif-ically, given an input feature vector x, the optimized circuit prepares a quantum state of 6the form 

|ψθ(x)⟩ , (1) which can be interpreted as a nonlinear embedding of the classical input into a high-dimensional Hilbert space. From this perspective, the PQC acts as a learned feature map, while the measurement and decision function determine how this representation is accessed for classification. Im-portantly, the same learned representation can be exploited in different ways depending on the choice of readout. In this work, we show that a feature map learned using one readout mechanism can also enhance the performance of an alternative readout applied post-training. This observation motivates the experimental analysis that follows, where we explicitly sep-arate the role of the learned quantum representation from that of the downstream decision mechanism. In what follows, we describe the two readout strategies considered in this work.  

> A. Linear head readout and decision function

We consider the standard setting where the expectation values of Pauli-Z operators are measured on each qubit. Repeated circuit evaluations allow accurate estimation of these quantities, forming a feature vector z = ( ⟨Z1⟩, . . . , ⟨Znq ⟩). A linear decision function is then defined as 

s = w⊤z + b, (2) where w and b are trainable parameters optimized jointly with the circuit parameters θ.In this work, we train this hybrid quantum-classical model using a softplus logistic loss, as described in Section IV.  

> B. Quantum Kernel SVM

The mapping x 7 → | ψθ(x)⟩ defines a quantum feature map that embeds classical data into a Hilbert space. The similarity between two inputs x and x′ can then be quantified via the inner product of the corresponding quantum states. This leads to the definition of the quantum kernel function 

K(x, x′) = |⟨ ψθ(x) | ψθ(x′)⟩| 2 . (3) 7which measures the squared fidelity between the two states. In practice, this quantity can be estimated using standard circuit constructions such as the inversion test. The resulting kernel can then be used within a classical support vector machine, yielding an SVM-QK. In this work, we first train a VQC with a linear head for binary classification. We then reuse the trained circuit (without the linear head) to construct the quantum kernel in Eq. (3) and train an SVM on top of this representation. Compared to untrained quantum kernels, this approach consistently improves performance in our experiments, as the feature map has been adapted to the task during the initial training phase. Intuitively, training with a linear head encourages the circuit to map inputs from dif-ferent classes to regions of the Hilbert space that are more easily separable under simple decision boundaries. When this learned representation is subsequently used to construct a kernel, the induced similarity measure reflects this task-adapted geometry, allowing the SVM to exploit a more structured feature space. Although this approach is simpler than more specialized quantum kernel training methods (e.g., [31, 32]), it nevertheless yields a significant improvement in accuracy compared to untrained kernels. We emphasize that this effect is empirical and depends on the interaction between the circuit architecture, training procedure, and dataset. Nevertheless, it highlights that a single trained quantum feature map can support multiple readout strategies, each revealing different aspects of the learned representation.   

> IV. EXPERIMENTAL FRAMEWORK A. Dataset and Protocol

We use the EuroSAT-MS multispectral land-cover dataset (10 classes) [7] and formulate classification as binary one-vs-one tasks over all 45 class pairs. For each pair, we adopt a fixed split of 1400/300/300 samples per class (train/validation/test). All experiments are repeated over five independent training seeds, with randomness affecting only model initialization and optimization (data splits remain fixed). For gradient-based models (NN and VQC), mini-batches are class-balanced. We report test accuracy averaged over the five seeds. For clarity, the main figures present mean accu-8racies across seeds, while 95% confidence intervals are provided in Appendix A. For class-level analysis, we aggregate pairwise results by averaging, for each class, the seed-averaged accuracies over all pairs involving that class. This protocol ensures a consistent and controlled comparison across models and class pairs. All quantum experiments are performed using noiseless state-vector simulation; hardware execution and noise-aware evaluation are left for future work.  

> B. Preprocessing

For each class pair, principal component analysis (PCA) is applied to reduce the input dimensionality to 16 components. The PCA transformation is fitted using the training data of the corresponding pair only, and then applied unchanged to the associated validation and test sets. This pairwise fitting ensures that preprocessing remains consistent with the task-specific training distribution while avoiding data leakage. E. Qubit-count sweep 

To examine how VQC performance varies with circuit size, we perform an additional qubit-count sweep using the same circuit family instantiated with nq ∈ { 1, . . . , 7} qubits. The goal of this experiment is not to introduce a different architecture, but to assess whether the trends observed for the main four-qubit model persist as the number of qubits increases under a closely matched design. The sweep is based on a direct generalization of the four-qubit PQC used in the main ex-12 periments. The same overall structure is preserved across all qubit counts: Hadamard gates on all qubits, symmetry-breaking Pauli-X gates on alternating qubits, an initial parameter-only layer of Ry and Rz rotations, and six repeated data re-uploading blocks. The main four-qubit circuit used throughout the primary experiments is therefore the nq = 4 instance of this generalized circuit family. As in the main architecture, successive blocks alternate between a nearest-neighbor ring topology and a longer-range entangling pattern adapted to the corresponding qubit count. This construction keeps the circuit family as consistent as possible with the original four-qubit design while enabling a controlled comparison across different system sizes. For a circuit with nq qubits and six re-uploading blocks, the total number of trainable circuit parameters is 2nq + 6(6 nq) = 38 nq, (13) corresponding to two initial trainable angles per qubit and six trainable affine coefficients per qubit in each of the six blocks. Thus, the four-qubit configuration used in the main experiments has 38 × 4 = 152 trainable circuit parameters, consistent with Table I. For this sweep, PCA is increased from 16 to 32 components so that larger qubit counts, especially nq = 7, operate on a sufficiently rich classical representation. Apart from this change in input dimensionality and the varying number of qubits, the evaluation protocol remains identical to the main setting: one-vs-one classification over all 45 class pairs with five training seeds per pair. For each qubit count, we first average test accuracy over seeds within 13 each pair, and then average across pairs. The reported 95% confidence intervals therefore reflect variation across class-pair mean accuracies rather than variation across training seeds. Finally, we note that while the Hilbert space dimension grows exponentially as 2 nq , the number of trainable parameters increases only linearly with nq according to Eq. (13). As a result, the variational circuit explores only a restricted subset of the exponentially large state space. This limited parameterization can lead to diminishing returns as the number of qubits increases, resulting in a saturation of performance beyond a certain system size. Conversely, scaling to highly expressive circuits with a number of parameters comparable to the full state-space dimension would introduce severe optimization challenges, including barren plateau effects [35]. These considerations highlight the need to balance circuit expressivity and trainability when scaling variational quantum models.

> C. Effect of qubit count on VQC performance

Figure 6 shows the average VQC test accuracy as the number of qubits is increased from 1 to 7, using the generalized state big circuit family described in Section IV E. Results are averaged over all 45 class pairs, with five training seeds per pair, and error bars denote 95% confidence intervals across the pairwise mean accuracies. A clear improvement is observed as the circuit size increases. The average accuracy rises from 92 .99% at one qubit to 94 .13% at two qubits, and then continues to increase more gradually up to 95 .18% at seven qubits. Overall, this corresponds to a gain of approximately 2.19 percentage points from nq = 1 to nq = 7. The largest improvement occurs between one and two qubits, whereas beyond three to four qubits the gains become progressively smaller, indicating a saturation trend. > D. Limitations

This study is based on noiseless classical simulation of quantum circuits and therefore does not account for hardware noise, finite-shot effects, or device connectivity constraints. In addition, the circuit architecture and classical hyperparameters are kept fixed to support a controlled comparison, rather than being extensively optimized for each class pair. Finally, the evaluation uses PCA-reduced spectral features rather than full spatial image structure, so the results should be interpreted as a study of compact multispectral feature representations rather than a comparison against large-scale remote-sensing foundation models or CNN pipelines.  

> VI. CONCLUSION

We presented a strictly controlled one-vs-one evaluation across all 45 EuroSAT-MS class pairs for multispectral land-cover classification, comparing VQCs to classical baselines under identical preprocessing, fixed splits, and five-seed training. Interpreted as compact nonlinear feature maps, the VQC consistently improves over linear baselines and remains competitive with a shallow NN on the same PCA-normalized inputs. A complementary qubit-count sweep further showed that, within the generalized circuit family considered here, VQC performance improves as the number of qubits increases from one to seven, with the largest gains occurring at small qubit counts and a more gradual saturation thereafter. Since this sweep was intentionally based on a simple extension of the working four-qubit architecture rather than qubit-specific circuit redesign, it should be viewed as a conservative estimate of the gains obtainable from larger quantum models. While an SVM-RBF remains strongest overall, constructing an SVM-QK (trained) from the same trained PQC yields a small but consistent improvement over the linear measure-ment head, suggesting that the learned quantum representation is more effectively exploited through similarity-based decision rules.
