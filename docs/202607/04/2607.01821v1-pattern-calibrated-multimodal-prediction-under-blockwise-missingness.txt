Abstract 

Blockwise missingness in multimodal data is usually treated as an incomplete-input problem. We instead focus on prediction for a prespecified observed-modality pattern, where the observed modality set determines the information on which the prediction rule can condition. A procedure that imputes missing modalities, zero-fills unobserved modalities, or trains a single pooled predictor may borrow information across patterns, but it can also mix pattern-specific prediction rules. We propose Multimodal Overlap-aware Shared-specific Alignment and Inter-pattern Calibration (MOSAIC), a pattern-calibrated framework for borrowing across missingness patterns without collapsing their prediction rules. MOSAIC learns shared and modality-specific representations, uses the available representations that overlap with the target pattern to fit a first-stage predictor, and then estimates the calibration gap from target-pattern data. We establish non-asymptotic bounds that decompose the error into overlap effective sample size, calibration gap, and representation-learning error, clarifying when cross-pattern borrowing improves over local fitting and when the improvement is controlled by rule mismatch or representation-learning error. Simulations examine representation recovery and target-pattern correction, and applications to ICU mortality prediction, emotion recognition, and glaucoma classification show gains when target-pattern samples are limited or pattern-specific rules differ. 

Keywords: Multimodal prediction; Blockwise missingness; Pattern-specific prediction rules; Target-pattern calibration; Representation learning. 

# 1 Introduction 

Prediction studies increasingly combine complementary data sources measured on the same subject (Baltrušaitis et al. 2019, Kline et al. 2022, Argelaguet et al. 2018). Electronic health records (EHRs) provide a common example: a patient’s record may include structured codes, clinical notes, and medical images, each generated by a different workflow and each 1

> arXiv:2607.01821v1 [stat.ME] 2 Jul 2026

Figure 1: Blockwise missingness induces pattern-specific prediction tasks. encoding a different aspect of the patient state (Krones et al. 2025). In practice, however, not every subject is observed on every modality. Entire modalities may be unobserved because of acquisition cost, workflow constraints, quality control, delayed release, or privacy restrictions (Rubin 1976, Little & Rubin 2019, Wu et al. 2026). The observed modality set determines the information on which a prediction rule can condition. In an EHR study, a code-only record and a code-note-image record may both be used to predict the same clinical outcome, but the latter task can condition on clinical text and imaging information that is unobserved in the former. Thus, blockwise missingness is not only an incomplete-input problem; it changes the observable prediction task. For an observed-modality pattern, the estimand is the best prediction rule among functions of the modalities available under that pattern. Figure 1 illustrates this distinction, and the formal definition is given in Section 2.1. Most existing approaches to missing-modality prediction focus on making incomplete records usable by a prediction model. Some reconstruct missing modalities through gen-erative modeling or multimodal imputation before prediction (Tran et al. 2017, Chartsias et al. 2018, Wu & Goodman 2018, Shi et al. 2019, Zhang et al. 2025). These methods may supply a usable input for prediction, but the completed modality is still inferred rather than observed. Others use zero-filling, masks, feature reconstruction, or fusion architectures that can accept incomplete modality profiles directly (Zhao et al. 2021, Yuan et al. 2021 a, Zuo et al. 2023, Wu et al. 2026, Zhang et al. 2026). Methods that learn shared and modality-specific representations further decompose multimodal information for fusion or missing-modality prediction (Hazarika et al. 2020, Lee & Pavlovic 2021, Wang et al. 2023). These approaches address the computational problem of accepting incomplete inputs. When used with a common prediction rule across patterns, however, they do not resolve the estimand problem: the fitted rule can estimate a pooled population quantity rather than the prediction rule relevant to a given observed-modality pattern. We refer to this discrepancy as bias from pattern-ignorant pooling: the bias induced by collapsing distinct observable prediction tasks into one pooled rule. A fully local strategy would avoid pooling across distinct prediction rules by fitting a separate model within each missingness pattern, but it can be inefficient when some 2patterns are rare. Fully observed EHR records, for example, may be much less common than code-only or code-note records. The methodological problem is therefore not whether to borrow, but how to borrow. A useful way to make this borrowing precise is to work with representations that separate information shared across modalities from information specific to each modality. Multimodal methods that learn shared and modality-specific representations (Hazarika et al. 2020, Lee & Pavlovic 2021, Wang et al. 2023) and statistical multiview decompositions (Lock et al. 2013, Feng et al. 2018, Gaynanova & Li 2019, Sergazinov et al. 2026) provide such a starting point: shared components can be compared across patterns, while specific components record which information is directly available. To address this problem, we propose Multimodal Overlap-aware Shared-specific 

Alignment and Inter-pattern Calibration ( MOSAIC ), a pattern-calibrated framework that combines shared and modality-specific representation learning, overlap-based first-stage prediction, and target-pattern calibration. MOSAIC first learns shared and modality-specific representations from unlabeled multimodal samples, so that the information shared by a source pattern and a target pattern is made explicit. For each target missingness pattern, MOSAIC then fits a first-stage predictor using the representations available in both the source and target patterns, thereby borrowing samples from related missingness patterns. Because this first-stage predictor is not generally the target-pattern rule, MOSAIC estimates the calibration gap using target-pattern samples. The calibration is therefore pattern-specific: it corrects the first-stage predictor toward the prediction rule for the target pattern. The resulting estimator borrows strength across missingness patterns without forcing the patterns to share one rule. Related questions have been studied in the statistical literature on prediction with missing inputs. Pattern submodels fit a separate predictor for each missingness pattern and remain valid even under missing-not-at-random mechanisms (Fletcher Mercaldo & Blume 2020), but fitting each submodel independently does not exploit information shared across patterns and can be inefficient when some patterns are rare. Stempfle et al. (2023) study a different route to efficiency by sharing parameters across pattern submodels through sparsity-inducing regularization while retaining pattern-level flexibility. These works concern partially observed covariates in a common variable space. Our setting is different: the missing modalities are heterogeneous modalities such as codes, text, and images, so cross-pattern borrowing first requires a representation in which overlap across modality patterns is well defined. MOSAIC therefore focuses on cross-pattern borrowing after constructing comparable representations for a target pattern, using first-stage prediction on coordinates shared with the target pattern and target-pattern calibration rather than coefficient-level shrinkage alone. Our contributions are three-fold. First, motivated by blockwise multimodal prediction, we formulate a pattern-specific cross-pattern borrowing problem: each observed modality set defines the information available for prediction, while borrowing from other patterns must not replace the target-pattern rule by a pooled rule. Second, we propose MOSAIC, a pattern-calibrated framework for borrowing information across different missingness patterns through their overlap in learned representation space. Third, we develop non-asymptotic guarantees that decompose the estimation error into the overlap effective sample size, the calibration gap, and the representation error. This decomposition explains when borrowing from overlapping patterns improves over local estimation and when the improvement is controlled by rule mismatch or representation error. The rest of the paper is organized 3as follows. Section 2 presents the proposed framework. Section 3 establishes theoretical guarantees. Section 4 reports simulation results, Section 5 presents real-data analyses, and Section 6 concludes.

2.2 Shared and Modality-Specific Representation Learning 

MOSAIC begins by learning shared and modality-specific representations that can be used consistently across missingness patterns. The shared representation is meant to capture information that is comparable across modalities, whereas the modality-specific representation retains information not carried by the shared part. This separation is important for prediction: if the representation available in a source pattern remains strongly associated with target-pattern information omitted from that source, the first-stage predictor may be shifted away from the target-pattern rule, as shown in Proposition 2. n + 1 , . . . , n + N , whose response labels are not used and may be unavailable. The simulations are organized around the two stages of MOSAIC. Experiment 1 assesses recovery of the shared and modality-specific representations under missing modalities. As ρ increases, the meth-ods separate more clearly: MOSAIC-pooled becomes less competitive, consistent with a growing mismatch between one pooled regression rule and the pattern-specific prediction rules; MOSAIC-local uses only the smaller set of target-pattern samples; and MOSAIC improves over MOSAIC (w/o calib), showing that the calibration stage corrects the re-maining difference between the first-stage predictor and the target-pattern rule. MOSAIC (w/o rep) converges more slowly than MOSAIC, suggesting that the learned shared and modality-specific representations help overlap-based borrowing use labeled samples more efficiently. Additionally, we compare MOSAIC with the raw-imputation baseline and extend the analysis to Gaussian, logistic, and Poisson GLMs under the same missing-modality protocol. The corresponding results are provided in Supplementary Section E.2. 

# 5 Real Data Applications 

We evaluate MOSAIC on next-visit ICU mortality prediction using MIMIC (Johnson et al. 2023). MIMIC contains longitudinal structured codes, clinical notes, and medical images, but these modalities are not available for every patient. These naturally occurring modality-availability patterns arise from routine clinical workflows, providing a real-world blockwise missingness setting for evaluation. Additional details on cohort construction, modality-specific preprocessing, training protocol, dataset statistics, and empirical missingness-pattern distributions are provided in Supplementary Sections E.3, E.5, and E.8. To examine the generalizability of MOSAIC across modality structures and task domains, we also evaluate it under controlled modality masking on IEMOCAP (Busso et al. 2008) and FairDomain (Tian et al. 2025). MOSAIC demonstrates advantages over the leading methods on both datasets. Full results and analyses are reported in Supplementary Section E.9.

Overall, these results show that MOSAIC’s empirical gains arise from the combined effects of cross-pattern borrowing, learned representations, target-pattern calibration, and pattern-specific final prediction, with the relative contribution of each component depending on the available modality pattern. 20 6 Conclusion 

MOSAIC provides a pattern-calibrated way to borrow information across blockwise multi-modal data without mixing distinct prediction rules. By separating representation learning from target-pattern calibration, it uses cross-pattern overlap to improve efficiency while keeping the final rule anchored to the target pattern. Our theory links the benefit of bor-rowing to representation error, overlap-dependent effective sample size, and the calibration gap, and our empirical studies show consistent gains when local samples are limited but calibration remains feasible. Several extensions remain for future work. First, the current theoretical analysis is developed under simplified representation models and missingness assumptions, while a more complete theory for nonlinear encoders and more general miss-ingness mechanisms, such as missing-at-random (MAR) or missing-not-at-random (MNAR) settings, would be valuable. Second, rare missingness patterns may still pose challenges when their overlap with other patterns is weak, and developing more robust aggregation or uncertainty quantification procedures for such patterns is an important direction. Finally, although MOSAIC is broadly applicable to multimodal prediction, further work is needed to adapt the framework to task-specific structures, such as survival outcomes, longitudinal modalities, or causal prediction problems.
