Abstract 

Few-shot multimodal classification commonly attaches a lightweight head, such as k-nearest neighbors, logistic regression, or a linear SVM, to a frozen pretrained encoder. Although computationally efficient, these heads can produce poorly calibrated confidence scores, limiting their reliability in calibration-sensitive applications. We evaluate TabPFN as a plug-and-play, zero-gradient classification head for frozen image, text, and audio encoders. Across 22,820 evaluation episodes spanning 14 datasets, 11 encoders, and three modalities, TabPFN achieves the best mean rank among nine classification heads on both negative log-likelihood (NLL) and expected calibration error (ECE). At a representative setting, it reduces NLL by 48–62% and ECE by 2.1–5.3 ×

relative to the average of the eight baselines while matching or exceeding their average accuracy. Its accuracy advantage is conditional, concentrating at moderate-to-high shot counts and low-to-moderate feature dimensions ( k ≥ 50 , d ≤ 32 ), and diminishing when labeled data are scarce, feature dimensions are high, or competing methods approach ceiling accuracy. In targeted backbone-adaptation experiments, replacing the trained linear head with TabPFN substantially improves calibration while preserving competitive accuracy. These results provide empirical guidance for using TabPFN as a training-free head in calibration-sensitive multimodal classification. To support transparency and reproducibility, we publicly release the source code, experiment configu-rations, and evaluation scripts in our GitHub repository: https://github.com/Jingxiang-Zhang/tabpfn-multimodal-embeddings. 

## Introduction 

Attaching a lightweight classification head to a frozen pre-trained encoder is a widely adopted pipeline for few-shot multimodal classification. The workflow is to embed data with a pretrained vision, language, or audio encoder; fit a head such as k-nearest neighbors ( kNN), logistic regression, or linear SVM; and deploy (Sun et al. 2019; Radford et al. 2021; B¨ ar et al. 2024; Abubakar et al. 2026; Wang et al. 2023). This gradient-free approach requires minimal com-putational overhead, enabling rapid deployment. Most classification heads share a systematic flaw: their confidence scores are poorly calibrated. Standard bench-marks rarely expose this issue because they focus solely on

accuracy. A classifier is well-calibrated if its stated confi-dence matches empirical accuracy; that is, a prediction la-beled 70% confident should be correct 70% of the time. However, these classification heads often fail to satisfy this property (Niculescu-Mizil and Caruana 2005; Dormann 2020; Silva Filho et al. 2023). In safety-critical settings such as medical imaging triage and clinical decision sup-port, a miscalibrated head is unsafe regardless of its accuracy rank (Pias et al. 2025; Rezaeian, Bayrak, and Asan 2026). Despite this risk, calibration across both frozen-encoder and fine-tuned adaptation pipelines remains underexplored. This lack of attention masks a fundamental technical chal-lenge: standard classification methods struggle to naturally produce calibrated probabilities. In zero-training pipelines, classical heads such as kNN, logistic regression, and lin-ear SVM are commonly used, but they are not explicitly designed to produce well-calibrated predictive uncertainty. Even methods that natively output probabilities, including logistic regression and kNN, can still produce miscalibrated probability estimates in practice (Niculescu-Mizil and Caru-ana 2005; Silva Filho et al. 2023). Linear SVMs are even more restrictive, as they do not produce probabilistic outputs at all. Likewise, introducing trainable parameters through linear probing or fine-tuning (e.g., LoRA (Hu et al. 2022)) typically improves classification accuracy but does not di-rectly optimize calibration. Consequently, classifiers built on both frozen and adapted backbones often remain poorly cali-brated (Guo et al. 2017; He, Chen, and Zhu 2023; Chen et al. 2023). To compensate for these deficiencies, post-hoc calibra-tion has become the standard remedy. For example, non-probabilistic classifiers such as SVMs are commonly cal-ibrated using Platt scaling (Platt et al. 1999), while prob-ability predictors are often refined using methods such as temperature scaling (Guo et al. 2017), isotonic re-gression (Zadrozny and Elkan 2002), or histogram bin-ning (Berta, Bach, and Jordan 2024). However, these ap-proaches introduce important trade-offs. Parametric meth-ods such as Platt scaling are limited by their calibration function (e.g., a sigmoid map) and may fail to correct more complex forms of miscalibration (Silva Filho et al. 2023). Accuracy-preserving techniques such as tempera-ture scaling require an additional optimization stage and a held-out calibration set, which is particularly problematic arXiv:2607.11007v1 [cs.LG] 13 Jul 2026 in few-shot settings. More flexible methods, including iso-tonic regression and histogram binning, often improve cal-ibration at the expense of predictive accuracy. Moreover, their effectiveness is highly inconsistent across classifiers and datasets (Manokhin and Grønhaug 2026). As a result, no existing post-hoc calibration strategy consistently deliv-ers strong calibration with zero additional training overhead for frozen-encoder pipelines. TabPFN (M¨ uller et al. 2021; Hollmann et al. 2025) is a promising candidate to fill that gap. By leveraging in-context learning (ICL), it takes labeled training examples as con-text to directly output predictive distributions over test in-puts, rather than optimizing fixed parameters. Because it learns to approximate these distributions during pretraining, the model yields natively calibrated probabilities by design. In this work, we use TabPFN v2.5 (Grinsztajn et al. 2025) as a training-free classification head applied to the repre-sentations of frozen multimodal encoders, which was the latest public release when this study began. TabPFN v2.6 and v3 (Grinsztajn et al. 2026) were released afterward and are compared post hoc in Ablation 3. The pipeline has four steps: encode → PCA-reduce → TabPFN forward pass →

calibrated class probabilities . Crucially, this approach re-quires neither gradient updates nor a held-out calibration set. While promising, TabPFN was trained exclusively on syn-thetic tabular data. Whether its calibration property transfers to real-world multimodal embeddings, and under what con-ditions its accuracy surpasses classical baselines, remains unknown. This paper provides a systematic empirical an-swer to both questions, making the following contributions: 1. Systematic evaluation. We evaluate TabPFN as a zero-gradient classification head across 14 datasets, 11 en-coders, and 3 modalities (image, text, and audio), com-paring it against eight alternative classification heads (Ta-ble 3). 2. Mapping the performance boundary. We establish the conditions under which TabPFN is optimal. We demon-strate that while its calibration advantage is highly con-sistent, its accuracy advantage is strictly conditional. Ac-curacy improves most in a regime of intermediate sample sizes and moderate feature dimensions, but this advan-tage diminishes when data is scarce, feature dimensions are too high, or baselines reach near-ceiling accuracy. 3. A zero-training correction for fine-tuning. We com-pare three pipelines: a frozen backbone with TabPFN, a fine-tuned backbone with a linear head, and a fine-tuned backbone with TabPFN. We show that replacing the trained linear head with TabPFN corrects the system-atic miscalibration typical of fine-tuned classifiers. This establishes a novel, training-free mechanism to achieve highly calibrated class probabilities on adapted represen-tations without sacrificing accuracy. 

## Related Work 

Calibration of Classifiers and Post-Hoc Remedies. 

Niculescu-Mizil and Caruana (2005) showed that maximum-margin methods produce sigmoid-shaped probability distortions; Dormann (2020) quantified that raw classifier predictions can deviate by up to 0.2 from true probabilities. Post-hoc methods such as Platt scaling (Platt et al. 1999), isotonic regression (Zadrozny and Elkan 2002), and BBQ (Naeini, Cooper, and Hauskrecht 2015) address this after training, but each require a held-out calibration set and an extra optimization pass; Manokhin and Grønhaug (2026) show no single method dominates uniformly. TabPFN requires no post-hoc step. Its calibration is an inherent property of its Bayesian training objective (M¨ uller et al. 2021), not of post-processing. 

Frozen-Encoder Classification. Training a lightweight head on frozen pretrained encoder representations is widely used for efficient few-shot classification (Radford et al. 2021; B¨ ar et al. 2024; Sun et al. 2019; Abubakar et al. 2026). Prior work on this paradigm focuses almost exclusively on accuracy, while calibration of the classification head is not evaluated (B¨ ar et al. 2024; Silva Filho et al. 2023). Our study fills this gap by systematically evaluating the calibration of TabPFN when used as a training-free classification head on frozen multimodal embeddings. 

Fine-Tuning and Calibration Failure. Fine-tuning pre-trained models achieves strong accuracy but often produces poorly calibrated outputs. Guo et al. (2017) showed that fine-tuned vision models are overconfident; He, Chen, and Zhu (2023) demonstrated that fine-tuning language mod-els degrades pretrained calibration; and Chen et al. (2023) confirmed that confidence increases continuously through-out fine-tuning regardless of correctness. Parameter-efficient methods such as LoRA (Hu et al. 2022) reduce compute cost but do not resolve the calibration problem. We show that af-ter a backbone has been fine-tuned, replacing its linear head with TabPFN substantially reduces calibration error without requiring any subsequent gradient updates. 

Prior-Fitted Networks and TabPFN. Prior-Fitted Net-works (PFNs) are models trained to approximate Bayesian inference in-context by repeatedly processing synthetic datasets drawn from a defined prior (M¨ uller et al. 2021). Rather than explicitly targeting the posterior during train-ing, PFNs minimize the prior-data negative log-likelihood of held-out examples. As proven by M¨ uller et al. (2021), this objective is mathematically equivalent to minimizing the expected Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the model’s approximation and the true posterior predictive distribution. Consequently, PFNs yield predictions that are well calibrated by design (M¨ uller et al. 2025), a property consistent with the view of in-context learning as implicit Bayesian inference (Xie et al. 2021). TabPFN (Hollmann et al. 2022, 2025) applies this framework to tabular clas-sification; TabPFN v2.5 (Grinsztajn et al. 2025), TabPFN v3 (Grinsztajn et al. 2026) further improve accuracy and cal-ibration on larger datasets. Helli et al. (2024) showed that TabPFN maintains stronger ECE than XGBoost even under temporal distribution shift. To the best of our knowledge, our study is the first to transplant TabPFN from tabular data to multimodal embedding spaces and characterize the con-ditions under which its calibration advantage transfers. Fine-tuning pipeline. The encoder backbone is adapted using three methods: linear probing (backbone frozen, only a linear head trained), LoRA (Hu et al. 2022) (low-rank adapters on the encoder’s attention layers), and full fine-tuning (all encoder parameters updated). For all three meth-ods, a linear head is appended to map the encoder output to 

C class logits. The active parameters, including the linear head, plus any unfrozen backbone or adapter weights, are then optimized using cross-entropy loss on an 80% subset of Dtr , with early-stopping validation on the remaining 20% subset. After adaptation, we evaluate two classification paths for each backbone: • Direct output: The trained linear head is applied to the adapted encoder on Dte , producing logits directly. • TabPFN head: The trained linear head is discarded. We apply PCA with a fixed dimension of d = 64 . This transformation is fit on the adapted encoder’s outputs from Dtr and applied to both Dtr and Dte . We then run TabPFN in-context on these reduced features. During this step, the adapted encoder remains frozen and requires no further gradient updates. It is worth noting that the path for applying the TabPFN head to a backbone adapted via linear probing is mathemati-cally equivalent to the frozen-encoder baseline. Because lin-ear probing updates only the classification head and leaves the encoder weights unchanged, discarding that trained head exposes the original, unaltered embeddings.

Evaluation Grid 

We evaluate a full grid across all datasets, encoders, and 5 independent random seeds, totaling 22,820 evaluation episodes. Within each episode, all classification heads are evaluated on the exact same train/test split. The grid param-eters are: • Shots per class ( k-shot): k ∈ { 5, 20 , 50 , 100 , 400 , 1000 }

• Number of classes: C ∈ { 2, 5, 10 }

• Feature dimension: d ∈ { 8, 16 , 32 , 48 , 64 , 96 , all }

To construct each evaluation episode, we aim to sample 

C classes and partition the data into k training examples and 100 held-out test examples per class. We enforce two data-sufficiency filters to ensure reliable evaluation. First, configurations requiring more classes than a dataset con-tains (e.g., evaluating C = 10 on a binary dataset) are glob-ally excluded. Second, if any sampled class contains fewer than k + 10 total examples, the specific episode is discarded to prevent statistically unstable test sets. For valid episodes where a class has at least k+10 but fewer than k+100 exam-ples, we maintain exactly k training examples and allocate the remainder to the test set. To prevent rank-deficient PCA projections, the final feature dimension is strictly bounded by min( d, kC −

1, d enc , 2000) , where denc is the raw encoder dimension and 2000 is TabPFN v2.5’s maximum supported feature capac-ity. For the d = all configuration, we omit the target d and simply extract this maximum allowable dimension. Due to the computational cost of gradient updates, fine-tuning evaluations utilize a targeted subset of this grid, de-tailed in section Experiment 3.

Experiment 1: The Calibration Advantage 

This experiment characterizes TabPFN’s calibration perfor-mance across the full evaluation grid. The analysis has two parts: a rank-based aggregation over all 22,820 evaluation episodes (Table 4) to summarize overall calibration perfor-mance, followed by a per-modality breakdown at a canonical setting (Figure 2) to show how large the gap is in practice. How the accuracy advantage varies with number of shots, feature dimension, and class count is the focus of Experi-ment 2. Across all valid evaluation episodes, TabPFN achieves the best mean rank on both calibration metrics: 2.12 ± 1.45 on NLL and 2.93 ± 2.00 on ECE (Table 4). The low standard deviation of these ranks indicates that this calibration advan-tage is highly consistent on average across the evaluation grid. On accuracy, logistic regression slightly outperforms TabPFN (3.20 ± 1.93 vs. 3.27 ± 2.01). However, because logistic regression ranks lower on both NLL and ECE, this demonstrates that strong accuracy does not inherently guar-antee well-calibrated probabilities. Tree-based methods rank poorly on both accuracy and calibration. Figure 2 shows the per-modality results at a canonical high-shot setting ( C = 10 , k = 100 , d = 96 ). We fix shot per class k = 100 to fully activate TabPFN’s in-context learning, and d = 96 because using all raw en-coder dimensions ( d = all ) degrades TabPFN’s calibration without improving other metrics. At this setting, TabPFN reduces NLL by 48–62% and achieves an ECE 2.1–5.3 ×

lower than the average of all baselines, while outperform-ing their average accuracy on every modality (+1.4 to +8.7 percentage points). This empirical result validates the mech-anism of TabPFN’s Bayesian training objective (M¨ uller et al. 2021): its predict proba outputs approximate true pos-terior predictive distributions, whereas classical heads return uncalibrated confidence scores that systematically deviate from empirical frequencies. Furthermore, per-head reliabil-ity diagrams (Appendix A, Figure 7) confirm this behavior; while the curves for linear SVM deviate significantly from the ideal identity line, TabPFN’s curve tracks it better across all three modalities. TabPFN’s calibration advantage does not extend to ASS. While kNN consistently achieves the tightest prediction sets (mean rank 1.18), TabPFN falls into the lower half of the evaluated methods on this metric (mean rank 5.51). Experiment 2: The Accuracy High-Performance Regime 

Experiment 1 showed that TabPFN ranks best on ECE and NLL on average, and is competitive on accuracy overall, but aggregate ranks do not reveal when it wins. In this exper-iment, we characterize which regimes, defined by shot per class k, feature dimension d, class counts C, and the dif-ficulties of the tasks, drive TabPFN’s accuracy advantage. Throughout this experiment, we compare TabPFN against the best of all eight competing heads. Figure 3 presents the four main findings; stratified means for all nine heads at 

C = 10 are in Appendix D (Tables 7 and 8), and the com-plete (k × d) win-rate breakdown and remaining metrics are in Appendices B, C, and E (Figures 8–10). TabPFN’s calibration advantage is unconditional; its ac-curacy advantage is not. The top row of Figure 3 shows an asymmetry between the two metrics. On ECE, TabPFN achieves lower error than every baseline across all k ≥ 20 

and all d ≤ 96 ; NLL shows an even stronger advantage, with TabPFN remaining below every baseline at all k and all d,including d = all (Appendix Figure 8). In terms of accu-racy, the result is different. At k = 5 , TabPFN falls behind logistic regression (0.742 vs. 0.787), as well as linear SVM, 

kNN, and the MLP, likely because five examples provide in-sufficient context for its in-context learning to fully activate. Starting from k = 50 , TabPFN leads or ties the field, holding the highest mean accuracy across the k = 100 to k = 1000 

range (peaking at 0.874). Along the feature dimension axis, the advantage is concentrated in low-to-moderate feature di-mensions (the yellow band, d ∈ [8 , 32] ). While TabPFN strictly leads in mean accuracy at d ∈ [8 , 16] , logistic re-gression marginally overtakes it at d = 32 . However, the win-rate curves (bottom row, first two panels) demonstrate that P (TabPFN > best-of-8 ) remains highly stable across this entire d ∈ [8 , 32] band before declining at d ≥ 48 . This confirms that even at d = 32 , TabPFN consistently wins just as many individual evaluation episodes against the best-of-8 baseline as it does at lower feature dimensions. Class counts C and task difficulty jointly determine TabPFN’s accuracy advantage. Splitting the win-rate curves by class count (Figure 3, bottom row) reveals that this ad-vantage scales with the number of classes. Across the low-to-moderate feature dimension regime ( d ≤ 64 ), the win-rate follows a clear ordering of C = 10 > C = 5 > C = 2 .However, this hierarchy breaks down at high feature dimen-sions ( d ≥ 96 ); because complex tasks reach TabPFN’s feature-capacity limits, the 10-class win-rate thus collapses. Binary tasks ( C = 2 ) never cross the 50% win-rate thresh-old because these simpler tasks allow the best-of-8 base-line to reach near-perfect accuracy, leaving no room for im-provement. This ceiling effect is explained in the two right-most panels: when the best baseline achieves ≥ 90% ac-curacy (57% of all episodes), TabPFN’s win-rate drops to 17.3%; across harder episodes (baseline < 90% ), it rises to ≈ 37% . Furthermore, increasing the number of classes provides TabPFN’s in-context mechanism with more multi-class structure to exploit. ## Discussion 

Although pretrained on synthetic tabular tasks rather than multimodal embeddings, TabPFN produces well-calibrated predictive probabilities when applied as a classification head to reduced multimodal encoder features. This advantage is strongest within the evaluated low-to-moderate dimensional range ( d ≤ 96 ); performance degrades sharply at the max-imum available PCA dimension ( d = all ), suggesting sensitivity to high-dimensional inputs. TabPFN’s distributed probability mass improves ECE and NLL, but also produces larger conformal prediction sets than the discrete, sharply concentrated vote fractions of methods such as kNN. The model’s accuracy advantage is governed by two bounding factors: it requires a minimum number of exam-ples ( k ≥ 50 ) to reliably estimate the multi-class poste-rior, and this advantage diminishes on easy tasks, where the best-performing conventional baseline already achieves near-perfect accuracy, leaving little room for improvement. Crucially, using TabPFN as an in-context learning classifi-cation head is highly complementary to gradient-based en-coder adaptation. While fine-tuning effectively adapts the backbone to complex or mismatched domains, replacing the trained linear head with TabPFN resolves the system-atic miscalibration inherent to standard, non-Bayesian deep learning classifiers at zero additional training cost. 

Limitations. This study has three main limitations. First, hardware resource constraints limited our fine-tuning eval-uation to a fixed training schedule on a restricted subset of encoders, without an extensive hyperparameter grid search. Second, because SVD-based PCA scales non-linearly with the number of training samples and encoder dimensions, fea-ture reduction becomes a computational bottleneck at large values of k and d. Finally, our findings apply strictly to discrete classification; these calibration benefits and perfor-mance boundaries do not necessarily generalize to regres-sion, structured prediction, or retrieval tasks. 

## Conclusion 

We demonstrate that applying TabPFN as a zero-gradient classification head on frozen multimodal encoders dramat-ically improves calibration, substantially reducing NLL and ECE relative to standard classical baselines. While this cal-ibration advantage is unconditional within the model’s fea-ture capacity, its accuracy improvements are concentrated in a specific mid-shot, mid-dimension regime. Furthermore, replacing a fine-tuned linear head with TabPFN provides a zero-training correction for the systematic miscalibration inherent to standard deep learning classifiers. Ultimately, these findings establish clear empirical guidelines for practi-tioners, highlighting TabPFN as a highly effective, training-free classification head for calibration-sensitive multimodal tasks. Appendix F: NLL and ASS for Adapted Backbones 

Figure 11 complements the Accuracy and ECE panels of Figure 4 by reporting NLL and ASS for the same backbones, modalities, and shots per class k. The NLL results mirror the ECE patterns: the direct adapted heads (light blue) gener-ally yield higher NLL, whereas replacing their linear heads with TabPFN (dark blue) reduces NLL toward the frozen-TabPFN reference (grey). The right summary panels show the same trend after grouping configurations by shot-count regime: the direct heads retain higher NLL from the low- to high-shot regimes, whereas the TabPFN heads reduce NLL to the frozen-TabPFN level or below. Conversely, ASS does not exhibit a uniform ranking across the adaptation methods, reflecting differences in conformal prediction-set efficiency.
