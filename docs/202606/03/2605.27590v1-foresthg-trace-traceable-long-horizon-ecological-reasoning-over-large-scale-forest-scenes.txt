## ABSTRACT 

Remote sensing question answering (RS-QA) aims to enable natural-language interaction with Earth observation data. However, most existing approaches generate answers directly from visual em-beddings, treating the task as a semantic prediction problem. In contrast, ecological analysis in large-scale forest environments often requires multi-step analytical operations, explicit computations over structured environmental entities, and reasoning processes that must remain traceable and verifi-able. This exposes a fundamental gap between conventional visual QA systems and the requirements of executable ecological analysis. In this work, we introduce ForestHG-Trace, a framework designed for executable ecological reasoning over large-scale forest scenes. The framework represents forest environments as multimodal scene hypergraphs, where trees, spatial units, and ecological relation-ships are encoded as structured entities that support higher-order interactions beyond pairwise scene relations. On top of this representation, a tool-augmented reasoning agent generates executable pro-grams that perform deterministic analytical operations, such as filtering tree populations, expanding spatial neighborhoods, aggregating physiological attributes, and comparing ecological groups, while producing explicit execution traces and evidence records. This design transforms ecological QA from direct answer prediction into an auditable analytical workflow. To systematically evaluate such capabilities, we construct ForestTraceQA, an executable benchmark for ecological reasoning that requires models to perform long-horizon analytical procedures over complex forest environments. The benchmark includes diverse reasoning tasks involving structural analysis, population statistics, and spatial-ecological relationships. Extensive experiments demonstrate that our framework enables reliable traceable long-horizon reasoning in large-scale ecological scenes and provides a principled platform for studying execution-driven environmental analysis with multimodal agents.

## 1 Introduction 

Remote sensing question answering (RS-QA) provides a natural-language interface for interacting with Earth observation data [ 1, 2, 3, 4, 5]. Recent approaches, often built upon remote-sensing vision–language models, mainly focus on short-horizon semantic answering, where models generate plausible textual responses directly from image embeddings [ 6, 7,8, 9 , 10 , 11 , 12 , 13 ]. While effective for descriptive queries, this paradigm becomes insufficient for ecological analysis in large-scale natural environments such as forest scenes, where answering a question often requires explicit analytical procedures rather than direct semantic prediction. In such settings, ecological questions involve structured reasoning over numerous environmental entities and attributes rather than simple semantic inference, exposing a fundamental gap between conventional visual QA systems and executable ecological analysis over structured environmental data. Ecological reasoning in forest environments typically involves long-horizon analytical processes . Answering a question may require explicit numerical computation, multi-step filtering, neighborhood-aware reasoning, and integrating

heterogeneous evidence derived from imagery, canopy structure, topography, and biophysical attributes [ 14 , 15 ]. For instance, estimating canopy dominance may involve identifying relevant tree populations, aggregating structural measurements, and comparing spatial groups under ecological constraints. In such scenarios, generating a plausible answer alone is insufficient; the reasoning process must remain reproducible, inspectable, and grounded in explicit evidence connecting conclusions to ecological observations and computations [ 16 ]. Consequently, ecological QA is better viewed as an executable analytical workflow rather than a purely generative prediction task. Supporting such reasoning requires an appropriate scene representation. Forest ecological analysis depends not only on attributes of individual trees but also on higher-order structures such as shared spatial units, neighborhood interactions, rule-induced semantic groups, and coupled ecological constraints. These relationships involve multiple entities and heterogeneous attributes. However, commonly used representations, such as flat tables or conventional scene graphs, mainly capture pairwise relations and struggle to express such higher-order ecological structures in a unified manner [ 17 , 18 , 19 , 20 , 21 ]. Without suitable representations, reasoning systems must approximate complex environmental relationships through shallow pairwise links, limiting their ability to support structured analytical operations. Moreover, even with structured scene representations, reasoning over large-scale ecological environments remains challenging. Forest scenes may contain tens of thousands of tree instances with heterogeneous attributes, forming extremely large and densely connected environmental graphs. Ecological queries often require combining symbolic filtering, numerical aggregation, spatial neighborhood reasoning, and semantic comparison within a single workflow. Performing these heterogeneous operations over large entity sets while preserving numerical accuracy and logical consistency makes ecological QA both a representation challenge and a scalability problem in reasoning over structured environmental data. To address these challenges, we model forest scenes as a multimodal geospatial scene hypergraph, where nodes represent tree instances and heterogeneous attributes, while hyperedges encode ecological units, semantic sets, and neighborhood structures. This representation enables higher-order ecological relationships to be expressed within a unified and queryable structure, providing a foundation for structured reasoning over large-scale forest environments. Building on this representation, we introduce ForestHG-Trace , a traceable framework for long-horizon RS-QA in forest scenes. Instead of free-form answer generation, our framework adopts deterministic tool-based execution: a Large Language Model (LLM) plans the reasoning process and invokes structured tools for reading, filtering, neighborhood expansion, aggregation, comparison, and auditing over the hypergraph. This design transforms ecological QA from answer generation into an execution-driven reasoning process that produces replayable traces and compact evidence records, enabling transparent and verifiable analysis. To systematically evaluate such capabilities, we further introduce ForestTraceQA , an executable benchmark for traceable long-horizon forest ecological QA. The benchmark organizes questions by execution difficulty and ecological task type, enabling evaluation beyond final-answer accuracy toward execution-aware assessment. Experiments show that long-horizon ecological QA is largely governed by execution complexity, and that combining hypergraph modeling with traceable tool execution substantially improves performance over single-step baselines and conventional scene-graph agents. Ablation studies further reveal that structural hyperedges form the backbone of ecological reasoning, while higher-order hyperedges provide additional gains for more complex analytical tasks. Overall, our contributions are threefold: • ForestHG-Trace Framework. We introduce ForestHG-Trace , an executable ecological reasoning framework for long-horizon RS-QA in large-scale forest environments. The framework models forest scenes as multimodal ecological hypergraphs and enables deterministic tool-based reasoning that produces replayable execution traces and verifiable analytical evidence. • Scene Hypergraph Representation. We propose a multimodal scene hypergraph representation that captures higher-order ecological structures—including spatial units, semantic groups, and neighborhood interactions— beyond pairwise scene-graph relations, enabling structured reasoning over complex forest environments. • ForestTraceQA Benchmark. We present ForestTraceQA , the first executable benchmark for evaluating traceable long-horizon ecological reasoning in forest scenes, supporting evaluation of both answer correctness and execution fidelity. 22 Related Work 

2.1 Remote Sensing VLMs and RS-VQA 

Remote sensing visual question answering (RS-VQA) introduced natural-language querying as an interface to Earth observation imagery, with early studies mainly focusing on scene-level semantic answering over aerial and satellite images [ 1, 2]. Subsequent work extended this line from coarse recognition to more structured scene understanding, including relational reasoning, counting, and multi-task modeling, as represented by EarthVQA, EarthVQANet, HRVQA, and related benchmark- or task-oriented models [ 3, 4 , 5, 6 ]. Overall, RS-VQA has evolved from image-level semantic prediction toward more compositional visual understanding, while still remaining largely centered on end-task answer generation. In parallel, remote sensing vision–language models (RS-VLMs) have developed from adapting general-purpose multimodal models to building domain-specific foundation models and instruction-tuned assistants. One line emphasizes grounded multimodal interaction and QA, represented by GeoChat, RS-LLaVA, Co-LLaVA, and H2RSVLM [ 7, 8, 9 , 10 ]. Another line emphasizes large-scale image–text alignment, domain pretraining, and instruction tuning for remote sensing, represented by RemoteCLIP, RS5M/GeoRSCLIP, ChatEarthNet, RSGPT, SkyEyeGPT, LHRS-Bot, and LHRS-Bot-Nova [ 22 , 23 , 24 , 11 , 25 , 12 , 13 ]. Collectively, these studies substantially improve multimodal understanding and remote sensing dialogue. Despite these advances in remote sensing QA and multimodal reasoning, existing approaches primarily rely on direct answer generation from learned embeddings and do not support executable ecological reasoning with traceable analytical workflows over large-scale environmental scenes. 

2.2 Tool-Augmented Reasoning, Agentic QA, and Graph-based Knowledge Organization 

A separate line of research studies structured and executable reasoning for complex question answering. ReAct, Toolformer, BINDER, and CHESS show that external tool use, symbolic execution, and intermediate reasoning programs can improve faithfulness and task completion on compositional problems [ 26 , 27 , 28 , 29 ]. In geospatial QA, MapQA further highlights the value of executable query generation over map data, while ThinkGeo begins to evaluate tool-augmented agents on multi-step remote sensing tasks [ 14 , 15 ]. This line shifts QA from purely generative answering toward execution-aware reasoning, but it remains only weakly connected to ecological scene modeling with explicit higher-order relations. Graph-structured knowledge organization provides another important foundation for such systems. GraphRAG and RoG show that graph-based indexing and path-guided reasoning can improve retrieval quality and interpretability, while HyperGraphRAG suggests that higher-order structures are beneficial when knowledge cannot be naturally reduced to pairwise links [30, 31, 32]. More generally, hypergraph learning offers a natural formalism for modeling higher-order interactions among multiple entities, attributes, and semantic groups [ 18 , 19 , 20 , 21 ], and provenance standards such as PROV-DM emphasize explicitly representing entities, activities, and derivation relations for reproducibility and auditability [16]. However, prior work rarely addresses remote sensing QA scenarios that simultaneously require higher-order ecological relation modeling, deterministic multi-step tool execution, and answer-level traceability. This underexplored intersection defines the problem setting addressed in this paper.

Each forest scene is represented by a set of complementary raster modalities that jointly describe forest structure, physiological condition, and local environment. Specifically, RGB imagery provides high-resolution visual information for delineating individual tree crowns and canopy texture. The canopy height model (CHM) encodes vertical forest structure and is essential for reasoning about tree size, height stratification, and stand complexity. Topographic layers—including elevation, slope, and aspect—characterize terrain-driven environmental gradients that influence species distribution, growth patterns, and stress exposure. To capture vegetation physiological status, we incorporate biophysical variables such as leaf area index (LAI) and fraction of absorbed photosynthetically active radiation (fPAR), which reflect canopy density, photosynthetic capacity, and overall vegetation productivity. These modalities are deliberately selected to support analytical ecological reasoning rather than purely semantic interpretation. Structural variables enable reasoning about forest architecture and competition, physiological indicators support health and stress assessment, and topographic context provides environmental constraints. Together, they form a multimodal observational basis from which higher-level ecological entities, groups, and relations can be explicitly constructed and queried in downstream reasoning. 

3.1.2 Scene parsing and entity alignment 

Long-horizon forest ecological QA requires reasoning over discrete tree entities rather than continuous raster fields. Operations such as neighborhood analysis, density estimation, stratified filtering, and rule-based grouping are ill-defined at the pixel level and therefore necessitate an explicit entity-centric representation. We parse each forest scene into two complementary components: an instance-level tree layer and a semantic environ-mental layer, reflecting the dual requirements of entity-centric reasoning and spatial constraint modeling. Individual tree crowns are detected from RGB imagery using DeepForest [40], producing explicit tree instances that serve as the atomic units for ecological analysis and reasoning. Instance-level detection is essential for defining per-tree attributes, neighborhood relations, and density-based computations. In parallel, environmental regions such as rivers, roads, and 4buildings are segmented using the open-vocabulary remote sensing segmentation model SegEarth-OV3 [ 41 ]. These semantic regions encode spatial constraints and management-relevant context that influence ecological processes but are not naturally attributable to individual tree instances. Together, the two layers provide a structured decomposition of the scene into reasoning entities and contextual constraints. All raster modalities are then spatially aligned to the detected tree instances. For each tree, structural, spectral, topographic, and biophysical measurements are aggregated from the corresponding raster layers, producing a unified attribute representation per entity. This alignment step converts heterogeneous pixel-level observations into grounded ecological entities, enabling subsequent reasoning to be expressed as deterministic operations over explicit tree instances. 

3.1.3 Hypergraph formulation 

Given a forest scene represented by multimodal raster observations, we construct an ecological scene hypergraph as the unified representation for downstream reasoning. The hypergraph is defined as H = ( V, E, X), where V denotes the node set, E the hyperedge set, and X the associated attributes. Scene parsing produces two complementary components: an instance-level tree layer and a semantic environmental layer. Individual tree crowns are detected from RGB imagery, yielding a set of tree instances Vt ⊂ V that serve as atomic reasoning units. Environmental regions such as rivers, roads, and buildings are segmented to form semantic spatial units Vs ⊂ V , representing contextual constraints not attributable to individual trees. All raster modalities are spatially aligned to tree instances. For each tree node vi ∈ V t, an attribute vector xi ∈ X is constructed by aggregating structural, spectral, topographic, and biophysical measurements from the corresponding raster layers. Hyperedges E encode ecologically meaningful groupings and higher-order relations. Each hyperedge ek ∈ E connects a subset of tree nodes ek ⊆ V t, defined by explicit criteria over node attributes, spatial relations, or rule-based constraints. These criteria induce group-based ecological structures such as spatial units, structural strata, physiological classes, density regimes, and interaction patterns. The resulting hypergraph supports executable reasoning through deterministic operations defined over (V, E, X).Filtering, aggregation, neighborhood expansion, comparison, and rule evaluation are implemented as composable operators acting on nodes and hyperedges, producing explicit intermediate states and execution traces. 

3.2 Deterministic Execution over the Scene Hypergraph 

Reasoning is formulated as a deterministic execution process over the scene hypergraph H, so that each reasoning step corresponds to an explicit and reproducible state transition. At step t, the reasoning state is defined as St = ( Ht, Ct),where Ht denotes the current hypergraph state and Ct represents the active reasoning context. The context explicitly records the currently selected node sets, hyperedge subsets, and intermediate results, which are required to support multi-step operator composition. Each execution step is specified by an operator invocation at = ( ot, ut), where ot ∈ O denotes a predefined operator and ut denotes its arguments. This formulation separates control from execution: the LLM selects the operator invocation at, whereas the semantics of each operator are fixed and independent of the language model. The execution semantics are defined by a deterministic transition function 

(St+1 , r t) = fot (St, ut), (1) which updates the reasoning state and returns a structured result rt. Determinism of fot ensures that identical operator sequences always induce identical state trajectories, making the reasoning process reproducible and auditable. The operator set O is fixed and closed, consisting of composable primitives defined over nodes and hyperedges, including read , filter , expand , aggregate , compare , and audit . Each operator corresponds to a well-defined ecological analysis primitive, such as tree-level inspection, ecological group retrieval, constraint-based subgraph construction, or subset comparison, rather than a generic graph manipulation. Restricting reasoning to this operator set guarantees that all intermediate states remain within the representational scope of the scene hypergraph. 3.3 QA Benchmark Construction 3.3.1 From ecological hypergraph to executable QA 

The lower-right part of Figure 1 shows how executable QA instances are constructed from the same ecological hypergraph introduced in Section 3.1.3. Instead of relying on free-form question–answer annotation alone, we instantiate controlled templates over canonical ecological primitives already encoded in the hypergraph, including spatial ecological units, forest structure, physiological health, density and competition, ecological relations, compositional groups, and rule-driven ecological conditions. In this way, each question is anchored to explicit ecological entities and higher-order subsets, rather than being written independently of the underlying scene state. 

3.3.2 Dual-axis organization of the benchmark. 

To make the benchmark interpretable from both procedural and ecological perspectives, we organize it along two complementary axes. The first axis is execution difficulty , denoted by hn ∈ { H0, H 1, H 2, H 3, H 4}, which controls the depth and structural complexity of the intended reasoning process. The second axis is ecological task type , denoted by cn ∈ { BP , SA , IA , MT , PO }, corresponding to Baseline Profiling, Stratified Assessment, Interaction Analysis, Multi-criteria Triage, and Pattern Optimization. The five task types reflect five common ecological analysis functions: BP for snapshot-style summaries, SA for stratified comparisons across factors such as health, slope, or density, IA for neighborhood interaction analysis such as competition and facilitation, MT for multi-evidence screening or diagnosis, and PO for search-oriented workflows such as threshold search, matrix scan, and best-pattern identification. 

3.3.3 Program DAG construction and gold execution. 

Each template is compiled into a program DAG whose nodes are canonical operators and whose edges encode dependencies among intermediate results. Rather than being manually written, the final answer is produced by executing 

Gn over the scene hypergraph through the same tool interface described in Section 3.2, together with the reference tool trace and intermediate states. This ensures that benchmark construction and inference share the same ecological state 6space and executable operator semantics, preventing the dataset from degenerating into a purely textual QA collection detached from the underlying scene representation. 

3.3.4 Traceable record schema and auditability. 

Each benchmark record stores a compact but fully executable provenance artifact set, including question ,

program_dag , tool_trace , intermediate_states , and final_answer . The final answer is additionally con-strained by fixed answer-schema contracts, so the benchmark remains machine-checkable rather than relying on loosely formatted textual responses. Therefore, ForestTraceQA evaluates not only whether a model reaches the correct final answer, but also whether it can reproduce the intended ecological reasoning process and its associated prove-nance. Detailed template definitions, skeleton instantiation rules, and answer-schema specifications are provided in the appendix. 

## 4 Experiments & Results 

To empirically validate the proposed ForestHG-Trace framework, we conduct a comprehensive set of experiments on ForestTraceQA. The evaluation focuses not only on final-answer accuracy but also on execution quality metrics. The benchmark is built from 2019 NEON airborne observations covering 20 sites, with 5 tiles selected per site, resulting in 100 forest tiles in total, as described in Section 3.1.1. 

4.1 Experimental Setting 4.1.1 Baseline Methods. 

We compare ForestHG-Trace with six baselines covering both single-step and multi-step reasoning. Single-step baselines include Vanilla LLM , Text-to-SQL , Vanilla RAG , and HG-Summary LLM , corresponding to direct prompting, structured querying, retrieval-based answering, and static-summary answering, respectively. Among them, Text-to-SQL follows the executable structured-querying paradigm in semantic parsing and text-to-SQL research [ 42 , 29 ], while Vanilla RAG follows the standard retrieval-augmented generation framework [ 43 ]. Multi-step baselines include Tool-Agent , a generic ReAct/BINDER-style tool-using agent over relational data [ 26 , 28 ], and Scene-Graph Agent , which replaces the hypergraph with an ordinary scene graph for graph-based retrieval and reasoning [ 30 , 31 ]. Together, these baselines cover unstructured prompting, structured execution, retrieval-based reasoning, generic tool use, and ordinary-graph reasoning. For fairness, all methods use the same LLM backbone, Qwen3.5 (397B-A17B). Implementation details are provided in the appendix.

Our primary metric is answer accuracy ( Acc. ), reported for each ecological task and overall. To better reflect the traceability of executable reasoning, we additionally report two trace-level diagnostics: execution coverage ( Cov. ) and trace similarity ( Sim. ). 4.2.2 Impact of hypergraph-based representation. 

Compared with other multi-step reasoning methods, the Scene-Graph Agent remains competitive on relatively simple tasks, reaching 40.69%, 36.18%, and 44.98% on BP, SA, and IA, respectively, but degrades substantially on MT and PO, where reasoning requires more complex multi-branch constraint composition and higher-order relational modeling. In contrast, ForestHG-Trace achieves 46.35% on MT and 27.04% on PO, surpassing the Scene-Graph Agent by 32.10 and 8.07 points, respectively. This noticeable gap suggests that ordinary scene graphs, limited to pairwise edges, cannot naturally capture the higher-order structures required for forest ecological reasoning, whereas hypergraph-based modeling provides a more suitable and expressive representation for coupled constraints, entities, and relations. 

4.2.3 Accuracy–efficiency trade-off. 

ForestHG-Trace also shows an efficiency advantage, requiring only 135.25 tokens per correct answer, compared with 557.49 for Vanilla LLM, 431.50 for HG-Summary LLM, 524.35 for Tool-Agent, and 355.37 for the Scene-Graph Agent. Although Text-to-SQL has a lower Tok./Corr. value (13.25), its overall accuracy is only 15.39%, with just 1.64% on IA, indicating that compact symbolic outputs alone are insufficient for this benchmark. Overall, our method achieves a substantially better accuracy–efficiency trade-off by combining hypergraph-based scene modeling with deterministic, compact, and verifiable tool execution. 4.3 Sensitivity to LLM Backbones 

Table 2 and Figure 3 report the performance of ForestHG-Trace with LLM backbones of different scales, aiming to examine how backbone capacity affects long-horizon ecological reasoning. 

4.3.1 Execution complexity as the bottleneck 

The results show that the main bottleneck of ForestTraceQA lies in execution complexity rather than ecological semantics alone. As shown in Figure 3(a), the average ground-truth trace length increases steadily from 2.75 ±1.29 on BP to 22.27 ±1.28 on PO, and this trend is accompanied by a consistent decline in accuracy across backbones. For example, Qwen3.5 (397B-A17B) drops from 75.59% on BP to 27.04% on PO, while GPT-5-nano declines from 61.65% on BP to 17.34% on PO. 

4.3.2 Execution mismatch across backbones 

Among all backbones, Qwen3.5 (397B-A17B) achieves the best overall accuracy of 54.45%, and also ranks first on every task-specific accuracy column, with 79.56% coverage and 71.91% trace similarity overall. This suggests that stronger long-horizon performance depends not only on executing more steps, but also on making correct decisions during late-stage filtering, branching, and aggregation. In contrast, although Mimo-v2 attains the highest overall coverage (81.29%) and trace similarity (76.71%), its overall accuracy is only 29.41%. The same mismatch is more evident on difficult tasks such as MT and PO, indicating that a plausible execution trace alone is not sufficient without correct decision-making at key steps. 

4.3.3 Under-execution in smaller backbones 

Figure 3(b) further supports this interpretation. Larger backbones generate trace-length distributions closer to the ground-truth distribution, whereas smaller models produce substantially shorter traces, indicating systematic under-execution on long-horizon tasks. This is also reflected in Table 2: Qwen3.5 (9B) achieves only 12.10% accuracy overall, with 16.57% coverage and 16.23% similarity, while Qwen3.5 (4B) reaches 21.40%, 35.24%, and 33.56%, respectively. Overall, these results show that scaling up the backbone improves long-horizon ecological reasoning, but the core challenge remains reliable execution under increasing depth and branching complexity.

4.4.2 Task-specific roles of different hyperedge families. 

The task-wise results in Table 3 further reveal different roles of the three hyperedge families. These results indicate that the gain of ForestHG-Trace does not come from adding more semantics indiscriminately, but from combining the right forms of hypergraph abstraction for different reasoning demands: BEH anchors the scene structure, CEH captures cross-attribute ecological coupling, and REH further sharpens these couplings into executable constraints.

## Accuracy vs. Tree Count / Tool Calls 

Figure 5: Accuracy versus tree count and tool calls. Performance is relatively insensitive to scene size but degrades substantially with increasing execution length. 11 4.6 Difficulty Scaling with Execution Load 

Figure 5 shows that difficulty is driven far more by execution load than by scene scale. In Figure 5(a), accuracy remains nearly flat (54–58%) as tree count increases from 0–5k to 25k+, suggesting that the hypergraph representation largely absorbs the complexity of larger scenes. By contrast, Figure 5(b) shows a sharp drop in accuracy as the number of tool calls increases, falling from 81.4% for 0–10 calls to 8.9% for 50+ calls. This indicates that the main bottleneck is not perceiving more ecological entities, but maintaining correct coordination over long, stateful reasoning chains. In other words, ForestHG-Trace scales relatively well in scene capacity, yet remains fragile under deep execution, where branching depth and late-stage decision errors accumulate. This further supports our central claim that long-horizon ecological QA is primarily execution-limited rather than perception-limited. 

## 5 Conclusion 

We presented ForestHG-Trace , a framework for traceable long-horizon ecological reasoning in large-scale forest scenes. By coupling a multimodal ecological hypergraph with deterministic tool-based execution, our method turns forest ecological QA from one-shot answer generation into an auditable reasoning process grounded in explicit intermediate states and evidence. We further introduced ForestTraceQA , an executable benchmark that evaluates not only final-answer correctness, but also execution quality and provenance fidelity. Experiments show that ForestHG-Trace substantially outperforms both single-step baselines and scene-graph agents, achieving the best overall accuracy while maintaining a markedly better accuracy–efficiency trade-off. Ablation results further indicate that basic ecological hyperedges provide the structural backbone of reasoning, while compositional and rule-induced hyperedges are especially beneficial for more complex tasks. More broadly, our results suggest that long-horizon ecological QA is fundamentally execution-limited rather than perception-limited: performance remains relatively stable as scene size grows, but degrades sharply as execution depth increases. This points to a clear direction for future work: improving planning, verification, and robustness under deeper and more branched reasoning chains for reliable ecological decision support.
