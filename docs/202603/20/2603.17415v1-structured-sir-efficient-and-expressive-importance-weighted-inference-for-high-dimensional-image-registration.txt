Abstract 

Image registration is an ill-posed dense vision task, where multiple solutions achieve similar loss values, motivating probabilistic inference. Variational in-ference has previously been employed to capture these distributions, however restrictive assumptions about the posterior form can lead to poor characterisation, overconfidence and low-quality samples. More flexible posteriors are typically bottlenecked by the complexity of high-dimensional covariance matrices required for dense 3D image registration. In this work, we present a memory and computationally efficient inference method, Structured SIR, that enables expressive, multi-modal, characterisation of uncer-tainty with high quality samples. We propose the use of a Sampled Importance Resampling (SIR) algorithm with a novel memory-efficient high-dimensional covariance parameterisation as the sum of a low-rank covariance and a sparse, spatially structured Cholesky precision factor. This structure enables capturing complex spatial correlations while remaining computationally tractable. We evaluate the efficacy of this approach in 3D dense image registration of brain MRI data, which is a very high-dimensional problem. We demonstrate that our proposed methods produces uncertainty estimates that are significantly better cali-brated than those produced by variational methods, achieving equivalent or better accuracy. Crucially, we show that the model yields highly structured multi-modal posterior distributions, enable effective and efficient uncertainty quantification. 

1 Introduction 

Dense image registration, establishing pixel-, or voxel-wise geometric correspondences between images, is a fundamental task in computer vision and medical image analysis. There is intrinsic ambiguity in whether or not images are matched so this problem is fundamentally ill-posed; there will be multiple plausible registrations results possible. This has lead to the use of probabilistic inference approaches both without Le Folgoc et al. [2016], Risholm et al. [2011], Simpson et al. [2015] and with the assistance of neural networks Dalca et al. [2019], Grzech et al. [2021], Siegert et al. [2024]. The use of deep learning has provided some benefits for medical image registration, particularly with respect to computational efficiency via amortised inference Dalca et al. [2019]. Nonetheless, existing approaches offer limited expressivity in their posterior distributions limited by the memory scaling of high-dimensional covariance matrices. Moreover, many approaches follow a variational paradigm with the strong assumption that the posterior is an (often factorised) Gaussian, making it impossible to capture multi-modal outputs. In this work, we propose a framework that relaxes the limitations of variational inference and simplistic posterior distributions. Our contributions include:

2 Background 

2.1 Registration Notation 

We denote the fixed and moving images as If and Im, defined over a common discrete spatial domain 

Ω containing Nv voxels; that is, we are learning the deformation that takes Im to If . The dense displacement field is stacked in matrix Z ∈ R3×Nv , where any column z ∈ R3 is a 3D displacement. The warped image is given by Im(x + z(x)) , denoted as Im ◦ Z for brevity. 

2.2 Probabilistic Registration 

While traditional registration provides a single MAP (Maximum-A-Posteriori) estimate, e.g. Ash-burner [2007], full probabilistic frameworks aim to characterise the posterior distribution p(Z|If , Im).This is particularly critical for medical applications, where confidence intervals can guide clinical decision making. However, Uncertainty Quantification (UQ) has been challenging for registration, in general, and for many approaches the characterised uncertainty has been found to correlate poorly with the needs of downstream applications Luo et al. [2019]; we seek to rectify this situation with well calibrated uncertainty and coherent posterior samples. 

2.2.1 Sampling and Approximate Inference 

attempt to capture this distribution using either: sampling based methods Risholm et al. [2011], Zhang et al. [2013], which tend to be slow for dense 3D registration; or variational Le Folgoc et al. [2016], Simpson et al. [2015], or Laplace approximation Wang et al. [2018], approaches that are both computationally intensive and only provide a unimodal approximation of the posterior. More recently, Grzech et al. Grzech et al. [2021] bridged the gap between deep learning and classical inference by employing Stochastic Gradient MCMC. While this allows for more robust posterior coverage than standard variational methods, the requirement to draw multiple samples at inference time maintains a significant computational overhead compared to single-pass amortized models. 

2.2.2 Amortised Variational Inference 

was introduced to resolve the computational expense of estimating an approximate posterior dis-tribution Dalca et al. [2019]. In this framework, a neural network is trained to predict directly the parameters of an approximate posterior qθ (Z|If , Im); typically a multivariate Gaussian. This is achieved by maximizing the Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO):

2.2.3 Uncertainty Calibration 

Although amortised variational methods are efficient, the resulting uncertainty estimates are often poorly calibrated, underestimating the posterior variance Cremer et al. [2018] and necessarily fail to capture multi-modal distributions. To address the multi-scale nature of registration uncertainty, 2PULPo Siegert et al. [2024] introduced a hierarchical Laplacian pyramid approach to capture uncer-tainty across multiple spatial scales. While PULPo improves UQ quality, its iterative/hierarchical nature add complexity to the sampling procedure. 

2.3 Importance Sampling 

Amortised importance sampling based inference was first introduced in the Importance Weighted Auto Encoder Burda et al. [2015]. A neural network is used to predict a “sample proposal distribution”, rather than an approximate posterior, where samples are weighted at test time based on their likelihood, prior and proposal probabilities. Importance distributions can be estimated in the same manner as variational methods, where we instead optimise the importance weighting:

2.4 Flexible Covariances 

Structured Uncertainty Prediction Networks Dorta et al. [2018], demonstrated an efficient amortised inference approach to a full-rank covariance via a Gaussian Markov Random Field Rue and Held [2005] spatially-structured, but sparse precision distribution. Low-rank covariance approximations have also been shown to be effectively learned using amortised inference Monteiro et al. [2020]. In this work, we integrate these formulation together into a more flexible parameterisation. 

3 Methods 

3.1 Sampled Importance Resampling (SIR) 

To approximate the complex posterior p(Z|If , Im), we employ the SIR framework Rubin [1987], Smith and Gelfand [1992], a form of importance weighted inference that approximates the objective in Eq. (2). While traditional approaches use all the samples from a proposal distribution to compute a weighted average, we draw inspiration from the resampling-based objectives in Maddison et al. [2017], which focuses the gradient computation on a subset of high-quality samples. Specifically, we use SIR to identify high-probability registration hypotheses from a large set of candidates drawn from the initial proposal distribution; these selected samples are then used to provide an informative supervisory signal for optimising the parameters of our proposal distribution network. This reduces the overhead of backpropagating through low-weight candidates, and permits a more expansive search of the proposal space. 3.3 Network Architecture 

We use a fully convolutional U-net style architecture, without bias terms. The model has 4 resolution levels, with 16 channels for the first resolution level of the encoder, and 24 channels thereafter. We have separate convolutional prediction heads for the mean, μ, the sparse Cholesky factor L, and the low-rank matrices R. The first two are calculated at half the voxel resolution and upsampled using a cubic spline, to improve efficiency, the low-rank matrices are produced at half that resolution level, and upsampled to match the Cholesky factor using bilinear interpolation. No activation functions are applied to μ or R; for L we use a soft-plus for the Cholesky diagonals, to ensure they are positive, and no activation for the off-diagonal terms. 

4 Experiments 

4.1 Implementation Details 

All models were trained for 50,000 steps with a batch size of 1 on a single NVIDIA RTX A6000 (48 GB VRAM), with a peak memory requirement of 32 GB. We utilized the SOAP optimizer Vyas 5et al. [2024] via the Heavyball library HomebrewML [2026], which facilitates stable training at high learning rates; we set lr = 2 × 10 −3 with β1 = 0 .9 and β2 = 0 .95 . A cosine learning rate scheduler was employed, decaying to 2 × 10 −4 after 30,000 iterations. To improve model robustness, we applied data augmentation consisting of coherent voxel shifts (up to 8 voxels) and independent (to each image in the pair) voxel shifts (up to 2 voxels). 

4.2 Hyperparameters 

We fixed the number of low-rank components to 25. The Cholesky connectivity pattern L accounts for neighbors within a 3 × 3 × 3 kernel and cross-channel correlations between deformation directions. We apply L2 regularization with a coefficient of 0.05 to the off-diagonal elements of L to prevent overfitting and ensure numerical stability during inference. We use a ratio of λμ = 2 .5, λ = 1 .0 and tuned the likelihood scale parameter to σ = 0 .5 on the variational baseline for segmentation accuracy while maintaining less than 0.05% folding of the deformation field. For the SIR-based variants, we introduce a temperature scaling hyperparameter to prevent the importance weight distribution becoming too spiky (dominated by individual samples), or flat (uniform sampling). We adopt a dynamic temperature scaling approachNeal [2001], where the unnormalised weights α are scaled by a factor of Tσα . Here σα is an exponential moving average of standard deviation of the weights, i.e. σtα = γ σ t−1

4.3 Inference and Posterior Characterisation 

At inference time, we draw Nl = 1 , 200 samples (in batches), and perform importance weighting on these. Through mutlinomial sampling, we then draw a total of Nk = 80 samples to characterise the posterior distribution. While we use the final EMA value of σα obtained from training for test-time scaling, we note that this could be optimised post-training to improve performance.

4.5 Data 

We use a subset of the OASIS Marcus et al. [2007] as pre-processed and described in Balakrishnan et al. [2019]. Each image has 35 automatically segmented labels using Fischl [2012], 17 of which are bilateral, and is resampled to 1 mm 3 isotropic voxels with an image size of 160 × 192 × 224 . The resolution of the predicted grid, following cropping some of the background, is 96 × 72 × 80 , leading to Nv = 552 , 960 and considering a 3D displacements field leads to us estimating a multivariate distribution over 1.6 million dimensions. The data contains substantial anatomical variations and differences in brain shape. During both training and evaluation, we perform subject-to-subject registration, where each image is registered to others within its respective set. For the test set, this results in 50 × 49 = 2 , 450 unique registration pairs to ensure a rigorous assessment of model generalisation and uncertainty calibration.

The spatial coherence, and multi-modality, of the learned distribution is exemplified by examining the improvement in segmentation performance by the Oracle sample. As shown in Figure 4, we can see segmentation corrections in smooth groups of pixels, which is enabled through our learned spatial distribution. To inspect individual cases, we performed PCA on the SIR sample deformations, run clustering to identify groups (in our case the top two) and then picked examples nearest to those cluster means. 6 Discussion 

Our results demonstrate that our proposed SIR approach, when coupled with structured covariance parameterisations, significantly improves the calibration of registration uncertainty without compro-mising accuracy. By moving beyond the restrictive assumptions of amortised variational inference, we demonstrate that registration uncertainty can be characterised in a manner that serves as a reliable proxy for anatomical alignment. 

6.1 Calibration and Meaningful Uncertainty 

A central finding in this work relates to the relationship between model uncertainty and segmentation error. Our proposed S+LC model significantly outperforms variational baselines in terms of calibration metrics, but also exhibits a strong negative correlation between label entropy and accuracy. The expanded entropy range of the proposed model, coupled with its superior registration accuracy, suggests that the captured uncertainty is highly structured within the high-dimensional deformation space, representing a coherent distribution of anatomically plausible hypotheses rather than stochastic noise. 9Figure 5: Illustration of the multi-modality of the posterior distribution, where disagreements between modes are marked in blue or orange. On the left, we find structured changes in the Thalamus where mode 1 has a DSC of 0.834 and mode 2 has a Dice of 0.824. On the right, we observe substantial differences in the left lateral ventricle, either on the interior or exterior border, with a DSC of 0.864 for mode 1 and 0.862 for mode 2. 

6.2 The Accuracy of Oracle Samples 

The selection gap between the SIR mean, and the Oracle provides an intuition into the complexity of the inferred posterior. It indicates that very good samples exist with high-probability in the proposal distribution, but perhaps due to multi-modality do not coincide with the mean. This finding supports the use of SIR for fitting complex posterior distributions. 

6.3 Computational Efficiency 

Despite the high dimension of the displacement field, our approach remains computationally efficient, requiring a single forward pass of the model to produce an effective proposal distribution. The entire process of proposal inference, sample drawing and weighting of 1,200 samples is completed in under 5 seconds on a single GPU. This represents a substantial computational benefit over hierarchical sampling approachesSiegert et al. [2024], which require forward passes through the model for each sample, or MCMC-based methods Grzech et al. [2021], which require multiple iterative passes. 

6.4 Limitations and Future Directions 

While our approach improves calibration, with good accuracy, there is a trade-off in terms of topological regularity and folding of voxels. Future work could investigate using stationary velocity fields, as in Dalca et al. [2019], to encourage diffeomorphic transformations. Furthermore, more complex regularisers Burger et al. [2013] may offer benefits in certain applications. Our architecture was chosen based on our existing code library, however the underlying SIR and covariance parameterisation are model-agnostic. Future directions could replace the convolutional backbone with a vision transformer architecture Chen et al. [2022]. In terms of improvement to segmentation calibration, we could investigate post-training optimisation of the temperature parameter. We could also include segmentation losses within the likelihood function; although these are inaccessible at test time, but they could contribute to learning the shape of the importance sampling distribution. Beyond medical image registration, our proposed integration of SIR with our structured sparsity and low-rank covariance model offers a versatile framework for high-dimensional spatial inference tasks 10 in inverse problems that suffer from ambiguity, such as optical flow Ilg et al. [2018], sparse-view CT or 3D image reconstruction and image super-resolution. 

7 Conclusion 

We have introduced Structured SIR, a framework for efficient and expressive approximate inference of posterior distributions in high-dimensional imaging tasks. We have demonstrated the application of this to probabilistic image registration, where we leverage SIR with a novel structured covariance parameterisation to characterise a 1.6 million dimensional posterior distribution of a 3D deformation field. Our proposed SIR algorithm makes use of a dual sampling pass: first evaluating a large set of candidates to weight based on their respective probabilities, then performing resampling of these to characterise the posterior. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first application of SIR to a dense vision task of this scale. Our results demonstrate that SIR with sufficiently complex covariance parameterisations, exhibits good calibration performance, substantially outperforming variational inference while maintaining accuracy. Moreover, the significance gap in the performance of the Oracle sample, confirms that our structured proposal distribution supports a diverse range of plausible deformations. This frameworks offers a scalable blueprint for uncertainty quantification in high-dimensional spatial tasks. While demonstrated here for registration, it could be extensible to other inverse problems where test-time evidence can guide the selection of solutions.
