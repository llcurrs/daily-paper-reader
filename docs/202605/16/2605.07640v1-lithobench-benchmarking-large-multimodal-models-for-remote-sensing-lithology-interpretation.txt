## Abstract 

Remote sensing lithology interpretation is fundamental to geological surveys, min-eral exploration, and regional geological mapping. Unlike general land-cover recognition, lithology interpretation is a knowledge-intensive task that requires experts to infer rock types from various features, e.g ., subtle visual, making reli-able automated interpretation highly challenging. Geological knowledge-guided large multimodal models offer new opportunities, yet their evaluation remains constrained by the lack of benchmarks that capture lithological annotations, multi-level geological semantics, and expert-informed assessment. Here, we propose 

LithoBench , a multi-level benchmark for evaluating geological semantic under-standing in remote sensing lithology interpretation. LithoBench contains 10,000 expert-annotated interpretation instances across 12 representative lithological cat-egories, including 4,000 multiple-choice and 6,000 open-ended tasks organized into five cognitive levels: Identification and Description , Comparative Analysis ,

Mechanism Explanation , Practical Application , and Comprehensive Reasoning .We further develop an expert-in-the-loop, knowledge-grounded semi-automated construction pipeline, coupling multi sub-processes, e.g ., structured geological image descriptions, to enhance geological validity and evaluation reliability. Exper-iments with multiple large vision-language models reveal substantial limitations in geological semantic understanding , particularly on higher-order explanation, application, and reasoning tasks. Taken together, LithoBench establishes a ded-icated testbed for advancing geological knowledge-guided multimodal models, supporting systematic model evaluation, domain-specific training and adaptation, and knowledge-enhanced reasoning toward expert-level lithology interpretation from remote sensing imagery. 

## 1 Introduction 

Remote sensing lithology interpretation is a fundamental component of geological surveys, resource exploration, and regional geological mapping [ 6, 12 , 36 ]. Its goal goes beyond recognizing surface targets. It requires the joint analysis of texture, tone, structure, geomorphic patterns, and geological context to infer rock types and their genetic implications [ 10 ]. This task differs from object recognition in natural scenes. Lithological targets are highly domain-specific and often visually subtle. Different rock types may share similar colors and textures. The same rock type may also vary greatly across weathering conditions, terrains, slope aspects, and imaging environments [ 6, 10 ]. As a result, lithology analysis requires models to not only perceive visual cues, but also understand their geological meanings. Rather than merely predicting rock categories, LVLMs enable more geologically grounded analysis, including feature description, lithological comparison, genetic interpretation, and application-oriented reasoning [ 16 , 37 , 52 , 53 ]. However, remote sensing lithology interpretation still lacks a dedicated and standardized benchmark for evaluating LVLMs [ 13 , 17 , 25 , 42 ]. This gap makes it difficult to assess models across both basic lithological recognition and higher-order geoscientific reasoning, while also limiting systematic comparison, diagnosis, instruction tuning, and task-oriented model improvement. Mainstream remote sensing datasets have provided important foundations for perception-oriented tasks such as land-cover classification, object detection, and semantic segmentation, with evaluation protocols typically centered on accuracy, IoU, and related task-specific metrics [ 21 , 32 , 40 , 41 , 45 , 54 ]. However, they are not primarily designed to assess the domain reasoning abilities required for geological interpretation. General multimodal benchmarks provide large-scale evaluation resources [ 23 , 31 , 48 , 49 ]. Yet their task design and knowledge structure are not well aligned with geological remote sensing, limiting their ability to evaluate professional-level geoscientific understanding. In lithology interpretation, key decisions rarely depend on a single salient object, but instead emerge from the joint analysis of texture, structure, geomorphology, hydrological evidence, and geological context. Therefore, benchmarks relying mainly on generic instruction templates are inadequate for this task. A discriminative and domain-faithful benchmark requires benchmark construction that is tightly grounded in lithological knowledge [14, 16, 25, 42, 51, 56]. 

Figure 2: Word cloud of LithoBench description terms. To address these limitations, we in-troduce LithoBench , a large-scale, multi-level benchmark for remote sensing lithology understanding. Un-like recent remote sensing VQA and RS-LVLM benchmarks (e.g., RSVLM - QA [ 56 ], VRSBench [ 25 ], OmniEarth [ 9]) that primarily focus on general land-cover or object-level understanding, LithoBench specifi-cally targets geological lithology inter-pretation, combining structured visual descriptions, domain knowledge re-trieval, and multi-level cognitive tasks to evaluate both visual perception and higher-order geoscientific reasoning. 

LithoBench is built from Gaofen-2 (GF-2) imagery and covers 12 representative lithology and auxiliary land-cover types. It supports two evaluation formats: multiple-choice assessment and open-ended generation, spanning five capability levels: Identification and Description, Comparative Analysis, Mechanism Explanation, Practical Application, and Comprehensive Reasoning. As shown in Figs. 1 and 2, we design a complete data construction pipeline to ensure domain validity and reli-able evaluation, consisting of high-quality image sampling, structured visual description, dual-source 2knowledge retrieval, hierarchical task generation, quality filtering, and model evaluation. By integrat-ing remote sensing evidence with geological knowledge, this design enables standardized assessment of both basic visual recognition and higher-order geoscientific reasoning. Using LithoBench , we systematically evaluate a wide range of open-source and closed-source LVLMs and conduct domain fine-tuning experiments [ 3 , 8, 16 , 24 , 28 , 51 ]. The results reveal clear gaps across capability levels: current general-purpose models remain weak in fine-grained lithological discrimination, genetic interpretation, and application-oriented reasoning. These findings highlight that remote sensing lithol-ogy understanding is not a direct extension of general multimodal evaluation, but a domain-specific setting requiring precise visual perception, reliable geological knowledge grounding, and multi-step geoscientific reasoning. The contributions of this paper are mainly reflected in three aspects: • We construct LithoBench , a multi-level vision-language benchmark for remote sensing lithol-ogy understanding, built from a large-scale set of GF-2 image samples and 10,000 high-quality interpretation instances. It provides a unified platform for evaluating LVLMs in visual description, fine-grained discrimination, genetic interpretation, and geoscientific reasoning. • We design an expert-in-the-loop, knowledge-grounded semi-automated benchmark construction pipeline that combines structured visual descriptions, geological knowledge retrieval, hierarchical task generation, and expert-guided quality filtering, enabling higher-order lithology reasoning grounded in remote sensing evidence and geological knowledge. • We systematically evaluate diverse LVLMs with LithoBench , revealing key limitations in fine-grained lithological discrimination, genetic interpretation, and geoscientific reasoning, and demon-strating its value for capability diagnosis, domain-specific training, and knowledge enhancement. 

## 2 Pipeline 

The construction pipeline of LithoBench is shown in Fig. 3(A). It consists of five stages: image preprocessing, structured description generation, dual-modal retrieval-augmented knowledge base construction, multi-level task generation, and quality filtering and official benchmark construction .We first crop GF-2 imagery into standardized patches, filter low-quality samples, and organize the remaining samples by category. A multimodal large model is then used to produce structured geological descriptions for each image, which provide stable semantic anchors for data construction. We further build a text knowledge base and an image knowledge base to supply geoscientific context and visually similar reference samples. Guided by five capability levels, namely Identification and Description (ID), Comparative Analysis (Cmp), Mechanism Explanation (Exp), Practical Application (App), and Comprehensive Reasoning (Rea), we generate both multiple-choice and open-ended evaluation tasks. Finally, candidate question-answer pairs are filtered through an expert-in-the-loop quality control process, which combines multi-dimensional LLM-as-a-Judge scoring, expert review, and quota-constrained selection, resulting in the official LithoBench benchmark. 

2.1 Data Collection and Description Generation 

In this section, as shown in Fig. 3 (A), we present the details of the Data Collection ( Stage 1 ) first about Remote Sensing Image Preprocessing . Then, the Description Generation ( Stage 2 ), consisting of Description Generator Selection and Description Generation and Quality Control , is introduced. 

Data Collection: The raw imagery of LithoBench is collected from GF-2 satellite images over exposed surface regions in northwestern China, covering diverse lithology-related materials and complex geological structures, as shown in Fig. 3(B). We define 12 categories: arenaceous rocks (AR), breccia (BR), carbonate rocks (CR), conglomerate (CG), crushed stone (CS), diorite (DI), gabbro (GB), granite (GT), gravel (GV), slate (SL), soil (SO), and water (WA). To improve label reliability, labels are cross-calibrated using field surveys, core sampling, and remote sensing interpretation, followed by expert inspection to verify patch-label consistency and remove ambiguous cases. The selected regions are cropped into 224 × 224 patches, and low-quality samples and edge artifacts are filtered out, resulting in 112,163 high-quality patches for structured description generation, knowledge retrieval, and QA construction. More details are provided in Appendix B.1 and Appendix B.2. With the processed samples, we then perform description generation. 3Figure 3: Construction Pipeline and Sample Gallery of LithoBench . (A) Detailed construction pipeline of the LithoBench . (B) Representative images of 12 lithology categories in LithoBench .

Description Generator Selection: We compare Gemini-3-Pro [ 11 ] and GPT-5.4 [ 34 ] through expert review and automatic evaluation, using Qwen3-VL-235B-A22B [ 4] as the judge model. In 937 blind pairwise comparisons based on professionalism, completeness, and evidence consistency, GPT-5.4 achieves a higher win rate than Gemini-3-Pro (55.92% vs. 44.08%). Expert checks on 200 sampled pairs also favor GPT-5.4 in 121 cases. We therefore use GPT-5.4 as the default description generator. 

Description Generation and Quality Control: To obtain structured representations for lithology understanding, we convert each patch into an expert-guided description . We use a semi-structured prompt template , designed with domain-expert input, to describe tone, texture, pattern, structure, hydrology, geomorphology, and spatial-scale cues in about 500 characters. Each output follows a unified “field–enumerated value–evidence phrase” format, balancing natural-language flexibility with automatic parsing and retrieval. Following this protocol, we generate descriptions for over 110,000 image patches. We then re-evaluate each image-description pair with GPT-5.4, which assigns an image quality score Siq and a description quality score Sdq to compute the final score S as: 

S = α × Siq + (1 − α) × Sdq + δ (1) where δ is set to +0 .3, 0, and −0.5 for “keep” , “manual review” , or “discard” , respectively. We set 

α = 0 .6 to emphasize visual evidence quality while retaining description completeness. Finally, we rank all image-description pairs by S and keep the top 50%, yielding about 56,000 high-quality pairs 

for geoscientific understanding instance construction. 4Figure 4: Quality Filtering and Official Benchmark Construction of LithoBench .

2.2 Dual-Modal Knowledge Base 

As shown in Fig. 3(A), we introduce the construction of the Dual-Modal Knowledge Base ( Stage 3). For explanation, application, and reasoning tasks, visible image cues are often insufficient, and external geoscientific context is needed. We therefore build two complementary retrieval sources [ 22 ]: a Text Knowledge Base and an Image Knowledge Base . The former provides geoscientific context for explanation, application, and reasoning tasks, while the latter is built from expert-curated samples to support similar-sample retrieval, fine-grained comparison, and realistic distractor generation. 

Text Knowledge base: The text knowledge base includes approximately 1,000 papers on petrol-ogy and remote sensing geology published within the past five years, together with more than 20 professional books on rock spectroscopy. We extract and chunk the documents, encode all chunks with BGE-Large-En-v1.5 [ 46 ]. During instance construction, the query combines the category label, structured description, and target capability level to retrieve the Top-3 chunks as geological context. 

Image knowledge base: The image knowledge base is expert-curated and contains 1,200 represen-tative samples, with 100 high-quality images for each of the 12 categories. All reference images are encoded with CLIP [ 38 ], and retrieval is performed by cosine similarity. Given a target image, the system retrieves the Top-3 visually similar samples from different categories. These references support fine-grained discrimination, realistic distractor construction, and Comparative Analysis. 

2.3 Multi-Level QA Generation 

Based on the revised Bloom’s taxonomy [ 1 ], we design the Multi-Level QA Generation stage ( Stage 4 of Fig. 3(A)) . Domain experts adapt these five capability levels to lithology interpretation by defining capability-specific question templates and expected reasoning focuses. This hierarchy covers a progressive understanding process, from recognizing visible lithological cues, to explaining their geological causes, and to assessing practical implications. It enables both overall model comparison and fine-grained diagnosis of failures in visual perception, knowledge grounding, and reasoning. For Identification and Description, and Comparative Analysis, instance construction mainly uses visual evidence. For multiple-choice evaluation, the system retrieves visually similar samples from different categories and combines them with the target image into a 2 × 2 composite image. These real distractors make the options more diagnostic than random choices. For open-ended evaluation, the system uses the target image and its structured description. Comparative analysis further adds the 

Top-1 similar sample to highlight key visual and geological differences. For Mechanism Explanation, Practical Application, and Comprehensive Reasoning, the system uses text-based retrieval. It retrieves the Top-3 knowledge fragments based on the image label, structured description, and target capability level. These fragments are combined with the image and template constraints to ground each instance in geoscientific evidence. This reduces unconstrained generation and makes higher-order instances more traceable. 

2.4 QA Quality Filtering and Official Benchmark Construction 

Following the analysis in Fig. 4, the automated generation stage yields about 270,000 candidate instances. These candidates vary in quality, image alignment, answer stability, and analytical rigor. They are therefore not directly used as the final benchmark. We apply an expert-in-the-loop filtering process that combines LLM-as-a-Judge quality scoring, expert review, and quota-constrained selection [ 19 , 30 ]. After automatic scoring, domain experts review representative high-scoring and borderline candidates, focusing on geological correctness, image-question alignment, answer reliability, and potential leakage risk. The expert feedback is used to refine filtering rules and remove candidates with ambiguous lithological evidence, unstable answers, or weak geological 5grounding.Details of the expert panel, review protocol, and validation statistics are provided in Appendix B.3. Each candidate is scored along multiple dimensions: question quality (QQ), image relevance (IR), clarity (CL), geological soundness (GS), answerability (AN), analysis quality (AQ), overall quality (OQ), and benchmark leak risk (LR). Distractor quality (DQ) is additionally used for multiple-choice instances. All dimensions are scored from 1 to 10. Since a higher LR means greater leakage risk, we define a low-leakage bonus as LLB = 11 − LR . Then, the final score for each instance is as: 

WeightedScore OPEN = 0 .26 · OQ + 0 .22 · QQ + 0 .18 · IR + 0 .08 · CL 

+ 0 .08 · GS + 0 .08 · AN + 0 .07 · AQ + 0 .03 · LLB. (2) The weighting scheme prioritizes semantic quality and visual grounding. OQ, QQ, and IR receive the largest weights because they reflect overall suitability, formulation quality, and image correspondence. CL, GS, AN, and AQ further ensure clarity, geological validity, answerability, and analytical support. The LLB term provides a leakage-risk adjustment. For multiple-choice instances, DQ is also included to favor plausible and non-trivial distractors. After scoring, we first select 15,000 high-scoring candidates through local deduplication within each capability-level and instance-type bucket. Domain experts then review these candidates based on both quality scores and professional experience, yielding the final benchmark of 10,000 instances, including 4,000 multiple-choice instances and 6,000 open-ended instances . Each of the five capability levels contains 800 multiple-choice and 1,200 open-ended instances. We also enforce global image uniqueness to avoid repeated images across the benchmark. A dynamic quota allocation strategy is used to fill under-resourced buckets while balancing instance quality, category coverage, format proportion, and image diversity.

4.2 Evaluation Metrics 

LithoBench contains both multiple-choice questions (MCQ) and open-ended questions (OPEN), so we use question-type-specific evaluation metrics. For OPEN questions, we report two scores. The first uses reference-based automatic metrics without LLM evaluation: 

OPEN w/oJ = 0 .6 × ROUGE -L × 10 + 0 .4 × BLEU -4 × 10 . (3) The second incorporates LLM-as-a-Judge: 

OPEN J = 0 .4 × ROUGE -L × 10 + 0 .2 × BLEU -4 × 10 + 0 .4 × JudgeOverallScore . (4) BLEU-4 (B-4) measures phrase-level n-gram matching, while ROUGE-L (R-L) measures longest-common-subsequence overlap and better captures global structure consistency. Details are in Sec. C.2. 

4.3 Result Analysis 

As shown in Fig. 6(A) and Table 2, LithoBench provides clear discrimination across models. For 

MCQ evaluation , small models fine-tuned with LoRA on the LithoBench training set outperform unfine-tuned open-source and closed-source models. This suggests that domain-specific supervision 

improves the use of lithology labels, visual features, and diagnostic cues. The gains are more evident in visually grounded levels, such as identification and description and Comparative Analysis, where objective accuracy is directly tied to fine-grained visual discrimination. For OPEN evaluation , automatic metrics and LLM-as-a-Judge scores show broadly consistent trends. However, JudgeOverallScore better captures differences in professional expression and reasoning structure. Some models obtain reasonable ROUGE-L or BLEU-4 scores but lower JudgeOverallScore. This indicates that lexical overlap alone does not guarantee complete , geologically sound , and logically coherent explanations. In contrast, stronger models may not always achieve the best automatic scores, but receive higher LLM-based judgments. Their outputs better match expected geological semantics, terminology usage, and reasoning organization. The capability-level results in Table 2 reveal a clear trend. Models obtain higher scores on Identifi-cation and Description, Comparative Analysis. Their scores decrease on Mechanism Explanation, Practical Application, and Comprehensive Reasoning. This indicates that current LVLMs can capture basic lithological cues in remote sensing images. However, they remain limited when tasks require geological knowledge grounding, genetic interpretation, or multi-step geoscientific reasoning. 4.4 Further Discussion 

As shown in Fig. 7, we select a multiple-choice question from the Identification and Description category to analyze the misclassification result of InternVL3.5-8B-HF (FT). The correct answer to this question is A: Diorite, whereas the model predicts B: Carbonate rocks. From the retrieved images corresponding to the four options, it can be observed that A and B are highly similar in overall visual appearance: both exhibit relatively continuous light-gray to gray-white exposed surfaces, moderate brightness, fine textures, fewer spots, and no obvious coarse clastic structures. Such similar tones and fine-grained textures can easily cause the model to misclassify the target image as carbonate rocks. 8Figure 7: Visual reasoning example for a LithoBench multiple-choice question. However, the correct option, Diorite, is more consistent with the target image. The target image shows a relatively homogeneous light-gray continuous surface overall, with only weak linear or weak banded patterns. It lacks the coarse-grained clastic features commonly seen in conglomerate and also lacks the darker, more mafic overall tone of gabbro. Meanwhile, compared with many carbonate rocks, this image does not show clear bedding or sedimentary banding structures. Therefore, this sample is more consistent with the remote sensing visual characteristics of diorite. This case indicates that the model error is not random, but is influenced by visually similar distractor options. The image RAG mechanism in this paper can retrieve candidate images that are highly similar to the correct answer in tone, texture, and spatial morphology, thereby constructing options with real visual ambiguity. This demonstrates that the multiple-choice questions in LithoBench 

not only evaluate the model’s category recognition ability, but also further examine its ability to distinguish fine-grained lithological differences and diagnostic geological features. Existing remote-sensing vision-language datasets usually obtain relatively high automatic evaluation scores. On the UCM-Captions dataset, RSGPT[ 16 ] achieves BLEU-4 and ROUGE-L scores of 0.657 and 0.783, respectively, while GeoChat[ 20 ] further improves these two scores to 0.807 and 0.791. On the RSICD dataset, although the scores are lower than those on UCM-Captions, GeoChat still achieves BLEU-4 and ROUGE-L scores of 0.574 and 0.563, respectively. In contrast, even after domain-specific fine-tuning, the best result of current models on the open-ended task of LithoBench 

reaches only 0.108 in BLEU-4 and 0.257 in ROUGE-L, which is substantially lower than those on existing remote-sensing vision-language datasets. This performance gap indicates, on the one hand, that LithoBench is not a simple general-purpose remote-sensing scene description dataset, but a high-quality and highly challenging benchmark constructed for expert-level lithological interpretation. Its task design involves fine-grained visual discrimination, geological terminology, lithological knowledge, and multi-step reasoning, thereby providing stronger professionalism and discriminative capability. On the other hand, the results also show that although current vision-language models have achieved promising performance on general remote-sensing scene understanding tasks, they still exhibit clear limitations when facing complex lithology recognition, professional geological semantic understanding, and reasoning-oriented question answering. Therefore, they are not yet capable of effectively handling such expert-level remote-sensing geological understanding tasks. Consequently, the relatively low automatic evaluation scores on LithoBench do not imply poor data quality; rather, they further demonstrate that LithoBench can effectively reveal the capability boundaries of existing models in professional geoscience scenarios and provide a more rigorous and valuable evaluation platform for the future development of remote-sensing geological large models. 

## 5 Conclusion and Future Work 

In this work, we introduce LithoBench , a multi-level benchmark for remote sensing lithology understanding. Unlike conventional remote sensing datasets that mainly evaluate category recognition, 

LithoBench covers five capability levels: Identification and Description, Comparative Analysis, Mechanism Explanation, Practical Application, and Comprehensive Reasoning. This design shifts evaluation from label prediction to geoscientific understanding of remote sensing imagery. We build 

LithoBench through a complete pipeline, including image preprocessing, structured description generation, dual-modal knowledge base construction, multi-level instance generation, quality filtering, and benchmark construction. We further evaluate multiple open-source and closed-source large vision-language models. The results show that LithoBench can distinguish model capabilities in both basic recognition and higher-order reasoning. Current models show initial lithology recognition ability, 9but remain limited in Mechanism Explanation, Knowledge Transfer, and Multi-step Geoscientific Reasoning. In future work, we will extend domain-specific fine-tuning experiments and develop an agent system for real-world geological remote sensing tasks. This will further improve model usability in practical lithology interpretation scenarios.

Limitations 

Question: Does the paper discuss the limitations of the work performed by the authors? Answer: [Yes] Justification: We add a section to discuss the limitation of our work in Sec. D. ## A Related Works 

Remote sensing lithology datasets are fundamental to lithology recognition, geological mapping, and resource exploration [ 10 , 12 , 36 , 39 ]. Existing lithology-related studies mainly focus on classification or geological unit mapping. For example, WorldView-3 imagery has been used for lithological mapping and compared with ASTER and Landsat-8 OLI data [ 47 ]; TASI thermal infrared hyperspectral data have been used for CNN-based lithology classification [ 29 ]; and ZY1-02D hyperspectral imagery has been combined with XGBoost for lithology classification [ 27 ]. PRISMA-based studies also demonstrate the value of hyperspectral data and machine learning for lithological mapping [ 39 ]. However, these datasets are usually supervised by category labels or mapping results, lacking question-answer annotations, open-ended geological explanations, and multi-level reasoning tasks. Beyond lithology-specific datasets, many remote sensing benchmarks have been developed for land-cover classification, object detection, segmentation, and geospatial foundation model evaluation, as summarized in Table 3. Representative examples include HyRANK for hyperspectral land-cover classification [ 7, 18 ], OpenEarthMap for global land-cover segmentation [ 45 ], FAIR1M for fine-grained object detection [ 40 ], and SAMRS for large-scale segmentation and detection [ 41 ]. Geo-Bench and PANGAEA further extend evaluation to multiple geospatial tasks and sensors [ 21 , 32 ]. Nevertheless, these benchmarks mainly evaluate visual recognition or pixel/object-level perception, and rarely examine lithological evidence interpretation, geological comparison, or mechanism-oriented reasoning. Recent remote sensing vision-language benchmarks introduce captioning, grounding, and visual question answering. RSGPT/RSICap focuses on remote sensing captioning [ 16 ]; EarthVQA, VRSBench, and RSVLM-QA provide remote sensing VQA tasks with different question types [ 25 , 42 , 56 ]; and broader benchmarks such as OmniEarth and MSEarth evaluate geospatial or Earth science multimodal understanding [ 9, 54 ]. Despite these advances, most existing vision-language benchmarks target generic remote sensing or broad Earth science scenarios rather than expert-level lithology interpretation. In contrast, LithoBench is specifically designed for remote sensing lithology interpretation. It contains 112,163 lithology-related image patches and 10,000 final question-answer pairs across 12 representative lithology and land-cover categories. Different from existing classification, mapping, captioning, or general VQA benchmarks, 

LithoBench evaluates models through both multiple-choice and open-ended tasks organized into five geological capability levels, enabling a systematic assessment of lithology identification, visual evidence description, comparative analysis, mechanism explanation, practical application, and comprehensive reasoning. 

## B Dataset Construction Details 

B.1 Image Source and Patch Preprocessing 

The raw imagery used for constructing LithoBench was collected from four Gaofen-2 (GF-2) satellite scenes over exposed surface regions in northwestern China. The study area contains complex geological structures and diverse lithology-related surface materials. We define twelve representative categories, including arenaceous rocks, breccia, carbonate rocks, conglomerate, crushed stone, diorite, gabbro, granite, gravel, slate, soil, and water. Representative examples are shown in Fig. 3(B). To improve label reliability, the category labels were not assigned from a single source. Instead, they were cross-calibrated using field geological survey records, 

21 core sampling information, and remote-sensing interpretation results, so as to reduce label ambiguity caused by visually similar but geologically different surface materials.The lithology masks were produced by integrating field geological survey records, core sampling information, and expert remote-sensing interpretation, and were then aligned with the GF-2 scenes for patch-level cropping. The original GF-2 scenes and their corresponding lithology masks were read as raster arrays using GDAL. Since the original images have large spatial extents and high spatial resolution, we cropped each image and its mask into paired 224 × 224 patches using a non-overlapping sliding-window strategy. For image storage, each spectral band was linearly normalized to the range of [0 , 255] , and the first three bands were saved as RGB PNG images; the corresponding mask patches were saved as single-band grayscale PNG files. Patches whose mask values were entirely equal to the NoData label were discarded, where the NoData label was set to 15 

in our implementation. Since a patch may contain mixed surface materials, we further assigned a patch-level label according to the dominant lithology in the mask. Specifically, a patch was retained as a single-lithology sample only when one lithology category accounted for more than 80% of the valid mask pixels. Patches that did not satisfy this threshold were excluded to reduce label noise caused by mixed lithological boundaries. After removing NoData-only windows, mixed-label patches, low-quality samples, and boundary-noise patches, we obtained 112,163 high-quality image patches, which served as the basic image pool for structured description generation, dual-modal knowledge retrieval, and subsequent question-answer construction. 

B.2 Lithology Categories 

The lithology categories in LithoBench were defined based on field geological investigation, core sampling records, and remote-sensing interpretation results. Instead of following a very fine-grained petrological taxonomy, we used an image-oriented category system that is suitable for GF-2 remote-sensing interpretation. The reason is that many fine-grained geological subtypes cannot be reliably distinguished from optical remote-sensing patches alone. For example, loose angular gravel, rounded gravel, and other gravel-like surface materials often show very similar tones, textures, particle-size patterns, and spatial distributions in GF-2 imagery. Therefore, they were merged into the broader gravel category. Similarly, coarse sand, medium sand, and fine sand usually present highly similar surface textures and spectral appearances at the patch scale, so they were grouped into the 

arenaceous rocks category. This merging strategy reduces unnecessary label ambiguity and avoids assigning visually indistinguishable subtypes as separate classes. Following this principle, we finally organized the dataset into twelve representative categories: arenaceous rocks, breccia, carbonate rocks, conglomerate, crushed stone, diorite, gabbro, granite, gravel, slate, soil, and water. These categories cover both major lithological units and auxiliary surface-cover types that commonly appear in exposed geological regions. Rock categories such as carbonate rocks, slate, granite, diorite, and gabbro were retained because they have relatively stable geological meanings and can often be associated with distinguishable visual or geomorphic patterns. Categories such as soil and water were included because they frequently occur in the same scenes and may affect lithology interpretation. Overall, the category design aims to balance geological validity and remote-sensing separability, ensuring that each class is meaningful for geological interpretation while remaining visually identifiable at the image-patch level. 

B.3 Expert Panel Configuration 

To ensure the reliability and geological validity of LithoBench , we organize an expert-supervised annotation and verification team consisting of three senior domain experts, five Ph.D. researchers, and ten graduate students. The expert team is involved throughout the construction process, including data preprocessing, prompt design, model selection, QA generation, and final benchmark filtering. During preprocessing, experts examine GF-2 patches together with lithology masks and geological survey information to remove samples with mixed lithology, unclear boundaries, severe shadows, weak exposure, or inconsistent label-image correspondence. During description and QA generation, experts refine all prompt templates and inspect the generated results to ensure that they follow the expected format, cover lithology-related cues, and remain geologically meaningful. For model selection, we jointly consider LLM-based evaluation results and real expert assessment to choose models that produce more reliable descriptions and QA instances. In the final filtering stage, experts review the 15,000 high-scoring candidates from multiple perspectives, including geological correctness, image-question alignment, answerability, reasoning validity, terminology appropriateness, distractor plausibility, and leakage risk. After this expert-guided verification, 10,000 instances are selected as the official benchmark. 

B.4 Structured Description Generation 

After obtaining the GF-2 image patches, we generated structured geological descriptions as intermediate representations for subsequent knowledge retrieval and question-answer construction. Before large-scale generation, we first compared the description quality of different LVLMs on the same lithological images. Given the original user instruction, the input image, and two candidate descriptions generated by Assistant A and Assistant B, a judge model was asked to compare the two responses and rank them from best to worst. The 

22 evaluation considered instruction adherence, visual relevance, geological accuracy, completeness, description granularity, and objectivity. As shown in Fig. 8, the judge prompt required a valid JSON output containing both an analysis field and a final judgement field, so that the pairwise comparison results could be automatically parsed and summarized. Based on this pairwise evaluation, GPT-5.4 showed better overall description quality than the compared alternatives, and was therefore selected as the default model for subsequent structured description generation and question-answer construction. For each retained image patch, we then used a structured prompt to generate a geological description. Instead of producing free-form captions, the prompt required the model to output a valid JSON object with predefined fields, including tone and color, texture, pattern and structure, hydro-geomorphological features, and spatial-scale cues. For each field, the model provided both a categorical value and a short evidence phrase grounded in visible image features. This format makes the descriptions more consistent, interpretable, and easier to parse automatically. As shown in Fig. 9, the prompt explicitly constrains the content scope and output format, which reduces uncontrolled generation and supports large-scale description generation with a unified structure. We further conducted quality control for the generated image-description pairs. The quality-control prompt evaluated each sample in two stages. The first stage assessed the visual quality of the image itself, including clarity, texture visibility, contrast and exposure, information content, structural or boundary visibility, and noise or artifact control. The second stage evaluated the generated description with respect to the image evidence, focusing on image-description consistency, coverage of visible features, specificity, evidence grounding, internal consistency, and hallucination control. This design separates image usability from description reliability, preventing low-quality images and inaccurate descriptions from being conflated. As shown in Fig. 10, the prompt required a structured JSON output containing image quality, description quality, and an overall recommendation. We ranked all image-description pairs according to the final quality score and retained the top 50% as high-quality image-description pairs for subsequent knowledge retrieval, question generation, and benchmark construction.

B.5 Dual-modal Retrieval Database Construction 

We first build a text RAG database and an image retrieval database to support candidate QA generation. For the text RAG database, we collect geoscientific materials from two main sources. The first source consists of recent papers published within the past five years and retrieved through Google Scholar searches using keywords 

25 related to petrology and remote-sensing geology. The second source includes classical domestic and interna-tional textbooks in remote sensing, geology, petrology, and geological interpretation. These materials provide professional terminology, lithological interpretation principles, and domain-specific geological knowledge. All documents are segmented into semantic chunks, encoded into dense vectors, and stored in a vector database. During QA generation, the retrieval query is formed by combining the target lithology label, the structured image description, and the target capability level. The retrieved knowledge provides causal geological context and interpretation principles, especially for explanation, application, and reasoning questions. For the image retrieval database, representative high-quality image patches are manually selected by an expert team from the full collection of more than 110,000 image patches. These selected samples are required to clearly represent the visual characteristics of their corresponding lithology-related categories and are then encoded into visual embeddings. Given a target image, the system retrieves visually similar samples from different categories. These retrieved images are not directly exposed in the final benchmark question, but are used internally to construct plausible distractors for multiple-choice questions and to strengthen comparative interpretation. This dual-modal retrieval design ensures that the generated questions are grounded in both visual evidence and geological knowledge.

To balance question format and capability coverage, two of the five capability levels are assigned as multiple-choice questions and the remaining three are assigned as open-ended questions. Therefore, each image contributes five candidate QA pairs with an MCQ-to-OPEN ratio of 2 : 3 , which is consistent with the final benchmark design of 4,000 MCQ instances and 6,000 OPEN instances. The assignment is deterministic rather than purely random: a fixed global seed and the normalized image path are used to initialize a deterministic random generator, ensuring that the same image receives the same question-type assignment across different runs. The generated assignment is saved as a JSONL plan file, where each record contains the image path, label, a mode_map specifying whether each capability level is assigned to MCQ or OPEN, and a seed_map specifying which question prototype is used for each level. The five QA generation workers then read this shared plan file and generate questions according to their assigned capability levels. Instead of using a single generic question-generation prompt, we design capability-specific guidance and question prototypes for each level. This design allows the generated questions to reflect different forms of lithological understanding, ranging from visual recognition and descriptive interpretation to comparative discrimination, causal explanation, practical assessment, and multi-step geological reasoning. For multiple-choice questions, visually similar reference images are internally used to construct plausible distractors. However, the final question is required to appear as a standalone single-image benchmark item, and no wording is allowed to reveal the existence, position, or layout of the retrieved reference images. For open-ended questions, the prompt encourages evidence-grounded answers based on the target image description and, for higher-order levels, retrieved professional knowledge. ## C Experimental Details 

C.1 Experimental Settings 

To validate the effectiveness of the LithoBench benchmark, we systematically evaluate multiple closed-source and open-source large vision-language models under a unified inference setting. The evaluated models include Claude-Sonnet-4.6 [ 2], Gemini-3-Pro [ 11 ], GPT-5.4 [ 34 ], Grok-4 [ 44 ], Qwen3.5-27B [ 4], Qwen3.5-397B-A17B [ 4 ], Intern-S1-Pro [ 57 ], InternVL3.5-241B-A28B [ 43 ], Kimi-K2.5 [ 33 ], and GLM-4.6V [ 50 ]. These models cover different sources, parameter scales, and architectural types, providing a broad comparison of current mainstream large vision-language models on remote sensing lithology interpretation tasks. In addition, we conduct LoRA fine-tuning on Qwen3.5-9B [ 4] and InternVL3.5-8B-HF [ 43 ] using the LithoBench training data, with each model trained for 3 epochs, to examine the effect of domain-specific supervision on remote sensing lithology interpretation ability [15]. All fine-tuning experiments are conducted in a Windows environment with an NVIDIA A800 GPU and an Intel(R) Xeon(R) Silver 4210R CPU @ 2.40GHz. The two LoRA training experiments use the same training configuration: batch size = 20 , learning rate = 1 × 10 −5, LoRA rank = 16 , and training epochs = 3 . The training set contains 196,348 samples, including 78,543 MCQ samples and 117,805 OPEN samples. To ensure the reliability of the experimental conclusions, the training set and the final benchmark set are strictly separated at the image level, with no identical image appearing in both the training and benchmark sets. Therefore, no image-level overlap exists between the training and benchmark sets, which reduces the risk of direct data leakage. In our LoRA fine-tuning setting, we adopt a parameter-efficient strategy rather than updating the full model parameters. The training script automatically searches for eligible Linear layers and injects LoRA adapters into the language-model backbone modules. Under the current training command, neither --train_vision 

nor --train_projector is enabled. Therefore, the vision encoder and the multimodal projector/merger are excluded from LoRA fine-tuning. The lm_head and embedding layers are also excluded. As a result, LoRA mainly updates the Linear layers inside the LLM backbone, including the attention projection layers 

q_proj , k_proj , v_proj , and o_proj , as well as the feed-forward network layers gate_proj , up_proj ,

31 and down_proj . The original model parameters remain frozen, and only the low-rank LoRA parameters are optimized during training. In the inference evaluation stage, all models use the same decoding parameters: MAX_TOKENS = 1024 and 

TEMPERATURE = 0 .0. This unified inference configuration controls additional fluctuations caused by different sampling strategies and makes the horizontal comparison across models more stable and interpretable. 

C.2 Detailed Evaluation Metrics 

LithoBench contains both multiple-choice questions (MCQs) and open-ended questions (OPEN), so we use question-type-specific evaluation metrics. For MCQs, evaluation is based on exact option matching between the parsed model output and the ground-truth option, with accuracy as the core metric. This metric directly reflects model performance in target recognition, diagnostic feature discrimination, and objective judgment. If the model output cannot be parsed into a valid option, it is treated as an incorrect prediction. For OPEN questions, we report two scores, OPEN w/oJ and OPEN J, as defined in Sec. 4.2. OPEN w/oJ is based on reference-based automatic metrics without LLM evaluation, combining ROUGE-L [ 26 ] and BLEU-4 [ 35 ]. BLEU-4 measures phrase-level n-gram matching, while ROUGE-L measures longest-common-subsequence overlap and better captures global structure consistency. The non-judge score assigns a larger weight to ROUGE-L because lithology interpretation often permits synonymous rewriting, varied sentence structures, and multiple valid expression paths. ROUGE-L therefore better reflects key semantic and structural consistency, while BLEU-4 provides complementary phrase-level precision and reduces overly loose matching. OPEN J further incorporates LLM-as-a-Judge evaluation. Automatic metrics are retained to preserve the objec-tivity and reproducibility of reference-based evaluation, while the judge score captures geological correctness, visual-grounding consistency, answer completeness, and professional expression quality beyond lexical overlap. For all OPEN evaluations, GPT-5.4 is used as the judge model, and the same scoring prompt is applied to all evaluated models to ensure consistency. For each sample, the judge model is given the question, the reference answer, and the model-generated answer. It assigns four scores on a 0–10 scale: correctness, completeness, faithfulness, and overall quality. Correctness measures whether the answer is factually and geologically consistent with the reference answer; completeness reflects whether the response covers the key conclusion, relevant evidence, and necessary reasoning; faithfulness evaluates whether the answer remains grounded in the given image/question context and avoids unsupported claims; and the overall score summarizes the final quality of the response. As shown in Fig. 14, the prompt enforces a strict JSON output format, which makes the judge results easy to parse and combine with automatic metrics.

C.3 Effect of Domain-Specific Fine-Tuning 

We also examine the effect of domain-specific supervision by comparing the original and LoRA fine-tuned versions of Qwen3.5-9B and InternVL3.5-8B-HF. After fine-tuning on the LithoBench training data, both models show clear improvements on multiple-choice accuracy and open-ended evaluation scores. This indicates that even relatively small LVLMs can better capture lithology-specific visual cues, terminology, and reasoning patterns after exposure to domain-specific supervision. The improvement is especially evident for multiple-choice questions, where the model needs to distinguish visually similar lithological categories. For open-ended questions, fine-tuning also improves the completeness and professional consistency of generated explanations. These results support the value of LithoBench not only as an evaluation benchmark, but also as a training resource for developing geological knowledge-guided multimodal models. 

C.4 Additional Qualitative Observations 

We further provide qualitative examples to better understand the strengths and failure patterns of different LVLMs on LithoBench . As shown in Figs. 15–19, the fine-tuned models generally produce more image-grounded and geologically consistent responses across different capability levels. For Identification and Description, the fine-tuned InternVL model accurately captures the gray-white tone, coarse mottled texture, weak structural fabric, and massive intrusive character of the diorite example, while some stronger general models provide broader but less specific alternatives such as granite, granodiorite, or metamorphic rock. For Comparative Analysis, Mechanism Explanation, Practical Application, and Comprehensive Reasoning questions, fine-tuned models more consistently connect visual cues with geological interpretation, such as using warm earthy tones to distinguish arenaceous rocks from crystalline surfaces, relating coarse texture and weak layering to granite or diorite formation, and assessing quarry suitability from weathering, blocky fragmentation, and joint-related concerns. In contrast, several non-fine-tuned models tend to produce plausible but less reliable answers, including over-generalized descriptions, unsupported biological or crystalline interpretations, and incomplete links between visible evidence and geological mechanisms. The MCQ examples in Fig. 20 further support this observation: in challenging cases with visually similar distractors, both fine-tuned models select the correct option, whereas the corresponding base models and many other models are misled by plausible alternatives. These results suggest that the difficulty of LithoBench does not only lie in recognizing image patterns, but also in grounding visual evidence in domain-specific lithological knowledge. They also indicate that domain fine-tuning improves both objective choice accuracy and open-ended geological reasoning quality.

## D Limitation 

Although LithoBench provides a dedicated benchmark for remote-sensing lithology interpretation, it still has several limitations. First, the current image data are mainly collected from GF-2 imagery over exposed surface regions in northwestern China. The visual appearance of lithological units may vary under different geological settings, climate zones, weathering conditions, vegetation coverage, illumination, and sensor characteristics. Therefore, the current benchmark may not fully represent all global lithological scenarios. Second, LithoBench covers 12 representative lithology and auxiliary land-cover categories. These categories are useful for evaluating common lithology-related interpretation tasks, but they do not cover the full complexity of geological mapping. More fine-grained rock types, mineral alteration zones, structural features, and mixed lithological units remain to be incorporated in future versions. Third, the construction pipeline uses large multimodal models and LLM-as-a-Judge modules for description generation, question-answer generation, and quality filtering. Although expert-in-the-loop design and multi-stage filtering are used to improve reliability, model-generated content may still contain biases, incomplete reasoning, or terminology inconsistencies. The benchmark should therefore be regarded as a carefully filtered evaluation resource rather than a replacement for expert geological field validation. Fourth, open-ended evaluation remains challenging. Reference-based metrics such as BLEU-4 and ROUGE-L cannot fully capture geological correctness, while LLM-as-a-Judge evaluation may introduce judge-model preference bias. To reduce this issue, we combine automatic metrics with judge-based scoring, but future work should further explore human expert evaluation and more geology-aware evaluation protocols. 

39 Finally, LithoBench focuses on patch-level interpretation rather than full-scene geological mapping. Real-world lithology mapping often requires multi-scale spatial context, stratigraphic relationships, structural geology, geochemical information, and field observations. Therefore, models evaluated on LithoBench should be considered as decision-support tools for remote-sensing lithology understanding, rather than autonomous substitutes for professional geological survey.

## F Broader impacts 

LithoBench is designed to support the development and evaluation of geological knowledge-guided multimodal models for remote-sensing lithology interpretation. Its potential positive impacts include promoting benchmark-driven research in geoscientific foundation models, improving the diagnosis of LVLMs in expert-level remote sensing tasks, and supporting applications such as geological survey, regional mapping, mineral exploration, and geoscience education. At the same time, this benchmark also has potential risks. First, automated lithology interpretation systems may be over-trusted by non-expert users. Remote-sensing-based lithology interpretation is inherently uncertain and should not replace field verification, geological sampling, or expert assessment. Second, models trained or evaluated on LithoBench may inherit geographic or sensor-specific biases, especially because the current data are mainly based on GF-2 imagery from exposed surface regions. Third, improved geological interpretation tools may be used in resource exploration, which could have environmental and social consequences if not governed properly. To mitigate these risks, we emphasize that LithoBench should be used as a research benchmark and decision-support resource rather than an autonomous geological mapping system. Practical deployment should involve domain experts, uncertainty analysis, field validation, and compliance with local environmental and resource-management regulations.
